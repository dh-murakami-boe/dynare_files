Heterogeneous Downward Nominal Wage Rigidity: Foundations of a Nonlinear Phillips Curve*

Stephanie Schmitt-Grohé†  Martín Uribe‡

This draft: October 13, 2025

Abstract

We propose a model with heterogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity across individual labor varieties. The model delivers a smooth, convex wage Phillips curve linking current wage inflation to current unemployment that is relatively steep at high levels of inflation and relatively flat at low levels of inflation. Using U.S. regional data, we estimate a smooth nonlinear wage Phillips curve that nests the linear case and find strong evidence of nonlinearity, consistent with the model's predictions. These results suggest that the cost of lowering demand-driven high inflation is low in terms of unemployment, and that the cost of reducing demand-driven high unemployment is low in terms of inflation. Although the model features occasionally binding constraints for individual labor varieties, there are no such constraints in the aggregate, making the model amenable to perturbation analysis.

Keywords: Downward nominal wage rigidity, nonlinear wage Phillips curve, unemployment, inflation, NAIRU.

JEL Classification: E24, E31, E32.

1 Introduction

The seminal empirical work of *Phillips (1958)* documented a negative relationship between unemployment and wage inflation and suggested the possibility that this relationship could be nonlinear, exhibiting a relatively steep slope at high levels of inflation and a relatively flat slope at low levels. The question of nonlinearity in the wage Phillips curve remained mostly dormant until the second decade of the 21st century, when two significant macroeconomic events rekindled interest: one involving a large reduction in unemployment without much of an increase in inflation (the exit from the global financial crisis), and another involving a reduction in high inflation without much of an increase in unemployment (the tightening following the inflation spike after the Covid-19 pandemic). More broadly, understanding the nature of nonlinearity in the Phillips curve is important because it can shed light on the possibility that both the cost of stabilizing high inflation in terms of unemployment and the cost of reducing high unemployment in terms of inflation may be relatively low.

This paper proposes a model of a nonlinear wage Phillips curve due to heterogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity. Specifically, wage rigidity is assumed to vary in intensity across a continuum of labor varieties. The nominal wage of each labor variety is bounded below by the average wage prevailing in the previous period times a variety-specific scalar. In all respects other than the heterogeneity of downward nominal wage rigidity, the model economy is standard; households and firms operate in competitive markets and are rational and forward looking.

In equilibrium the model delivers a nonlinear wage Phillips curve. An increase in wage inflation raises the fraction of labor varieties that are not constrained by the wage lower bound. As a result, the fraction of the labor force suffering involuntary unemployment falls. These effects imply a negative relationship between current wage inflation and current unemployment. Importantly, the sensitivity of the implied relationship between unemployment and wage inflation changes at different levels of inflation. For low levels of inflation, a large measure of workers is stuck at their wage lower bound. As a result, an increase in inflation, by lowering the real value of the wage lower bound, raises employment for a large measure of workers. Thus, equilibrium unemployment is relatively sensitive to changes in inflation. By contrast, for high levels of inflation, the mass of workers with a binding wage constraint is small, so an increase in inflation stimulates employment, but only for a small group of workers, rendering unemployment relatively insensitive to changes in inflation.

A conceptual contribution of this paper is to show that market power and price setting are not prerequisites for introducing nominal rigidity. The model does not make the standard assumption that workers possess market power and instead treats households and firms

## Page 3

as price takers in both product and factor markets. The implied Phillips curve is a contemporaneous relationship between unemployment and wage inflation, in line with Phillips’ empirical formulation. In this respect, the proposed framework departs from the New Keynesian model, which features a forward-looking Phillips curve linking unemployment not only to current wage inflation but also to future expected wage inflation. The reason why the new-Keynesian model generates an expectations-augmented wage Phillips curve is that it assumes that workers have market power. This assumption together with the assumption of nominal wage rigidity implies that the wage setting decision is forward looking, as today’s nominal wage choice impacts the entire expected future path of the worker’s real wage. The assumption that workers have market power can be justified in economies with a strong presence of labor unions, but is less tenable in economies, like the United States, in which secularly a small fraction of the labor force is unionized.

We identify the parameters of the model using moments stemming from both macro and micro data that include no information about the curvature of the wage Phillips curve. The calibrated model predicts a wage Phillips curve that is smooth and nonlinear. We stress the smoothness of the implied nonlinear Phillips curve in light of econometric evidence suggesting the lack of a kink in the relationship between wage growth and unemployment *(Bernanke and Blanchard, 2023)*. The implied degree of nonlinearity of the wage Phillips curve is quantitatively significant. For example, reducing inflation from 6 to 5 percent, raises unemployment by 0.3 percentage points, while reducing inflation from 2 to 1 percent raises unemployment by 3 percentage points.

We find that the predicted Phillips curve captures relatively well the nonlinear relationship between unemployment and nominal wage growth observed in the U.S. economy over the past four decades. In particular, it provides theoretical support for the relatively low cost in terms of employment resulting from the stabilization efforts in the aftermath of the Covid-19 inflation spike as well as for the missing inflation observed during the recovery from the 2008 financial crisis. For the pandemic era, the model predicts that in 2020 and 2021, the U.S. economy was hit by adverse supply shocks, but that the inflation spike of 2022 was primarily due to demand shocks.

We explore the smooth nonlinearity of the Phillips curve further by estimating a parametric nonlinear wage Phillips curve that nests a linear version as a special case using regional U.S. data from combined statistical areas (CSAs). We find statistically significant evidence of a nonlinear component. A version of the model calibrated to match the location and slope, but not the curvature, of the regional Phillips curve generates a degree of nonlinearity consistent with the estimated empirical relationship.

In spite of the aforementioned differences with the new-Keynesian framework, for regular

fluctuations of inflation around the intended target, under plausible calibrations, the proposed model delivers equilibrium dynamics that are quantitatively similar to those associated with the standard new-Keynesian model with wage rigidity. An implication of this result is that the assumption that workers have market power does not appear to play a crucial role, at least for standard calibrations of the model considered in the related literature. In sum, the proposed model globally delivers a nonlinear Phillips curve—which is important for understanding unusual events like the pandemic inflation and the great contraction—and locally preserves the dynamic properties of the new-Keynesian model—which is important for understanding normal fluctuations like those observed during the great moderation (roughly 1985 to 2007).

Finally, the paper makes a methodological contribution. One impediment that has limited a more widespread adoption of models with downward nominal wage rigidity in monetary analysis in spite of their empirical appeal, is the difficulty to approximate their equilibrium conditions due to the presence of occasionally binding constraints. This is most relevant for medium scale models used for policy analysis. This paper contributes to overcoming this impediment. Unlike standard models with homogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity, the proposed model is amenable to perturbation analysis, which is the standard method used to approximate and estimate equilibrium dynamics. Although in the present formulation there are occasionally binding constraints at the level of individual labor varieties, in the aggregate the equilibrium conditions do not feature such restrictions, thereby allowing for the differentiation of the aggregate equilibrium conditions around the deterministic steady-state.

Related Literature
The empirical relevance of downward nominal wage rigidity has been extensively documented by, among others, *Card and Hyslop (1997)*, *Kahn (1997)*, *Altonji and Devereux (2000)*, *Gottschalk (2005)*, *Barattieri, Basu, and Gottschalk (2014)*, *Daly and Hobijn (2014)*, *Schmitt-Grohé and Uribe (2016)*, *Jo (2025)*, and *Grigsby, Hurst, and Yildirmaz (2021)*.

Cross-sectional heterogeneity in the degree of downward nominal wage rigidity, the friction that gives rise to a convex wage Phillips curve in the model proposed in this paper, has been documented using administrative, survey, and experimental data. Using data on nominal wage changes from 1991 to 1997 from a Swiss representative labor force survey and a representative administrative database, *Fehr and Goette (2005)* estimate significant dispersion in the cost of cutting nominal wages across individual workers. This finding is consistent with experimental results by *Fehr and Falk (1999)* and *Fehr and Gächter (2000)* showing that respondents vary with respect to their nominal fairness standards. In turn, *Bewley (1999)* finds that fairness considerations are the main reason why firms are reluctant to cut wages during recessions. Specifically, Bewley conducts a survey of more than 300 firm

managers in the northeastern United States during the recession of the early 1990s and finds that the most common answer of why firms were reluctant to cut wages was that wage cuts undermine morale, which disrupts productivity in the workplace.

There is also evidence of heterogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity at the layoff margin. *Davis and Krolikowski (2024)* conduct a survey of new unemployment insurance recipients in Illinois over the period September 2018 to July 2019, during which the labor market was relatively tight. They find that survey respondents varied significantly in the nominal wage cut that they would have been willing to accept to maintain their last job, even when controlling for individual characteristics including race, gender, education, and job tenure.

Heterogeneity in nominal wage rigidity stemming from the fact that the timing of wage changes varies across firms and sectors and is determined independently of the state of the business cycle has been documented using large representative administrative data sets *(see Murray, 2021, for the United States; Faia and Pezone, 2023, and Fanfani, 2023, for Italy; and Adamopoulou, Díez-Catalán, and Villanueva, 2024, for Spain)*. All of these studies demonstrate that heterogeneity is substantial and has significant real effects. To the best of our knowledge, the present paper is the first one to introduce heterogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity into a general equilibrium setting.

This paper is also related to a literature on the role of nominal wage rigidity for macroeconomic adjustment. In the context of the new-Keynesian framework, sticky wages à la Calvo was introduced by *Erceg, Henderson, and Levin (2000)*. The derivation of a linear wage Phillips curve associated with that model is presented in *Casares (2010)* and *Galí (2011)*. *Kim and Ruge-Murcia (2009)* study a model with nominal wage rigidity à la *Rotemberg (1982)* with an asymmetric wage adjustment cost function. They estimate the parameters of this cost function and find that wage cuts are more costly than wage increases. They estimate that downward nominal wage rigidity implies a positive optimal inflation rate of about 35 basis points. *Elsby (2009)* studies downward nominal wage rigidity in the context of a model in which firms have monopsony power in the labor market and *Benigno and Ricci (2011)* in a model in which workers have monopoly power. There is also a literature combining labor search frictions and nominal rigidities including *Faia (2008)*, *Gertler, Sala, and Trigari (2008)*, and *Dupraz, Nakamura, and Steinsson (2022)*. Unlike the present study, these papers are not concerned with the global nonlinearity of the Phillips curve.

There is a literature that has examined the effects of nominal wage rigidity more globally. The starting point is the empirical estimate by *Phillips (1958)* of a negative nonlinear relation between wage inflation and unemployment. ## 2 The Model

The model features firms that use a variety of differentiated labor inputs. Nominal wages are downwardly rigid and the degree of rigidity varies across labor varieties. Households and firms are price takers in product and factor markets. Like existing models with nominal rigidity, the adjustment is non-Walrasian. A difference with existing models in the new-Keynesian tradition is that nominal rigidity is introduced without requiring market power and price setting.

### 2.1 Firms

Firms use labor to produce a final good. Profits are given by

$P_{t}a_{t}F(h_{t})-W_{t}h_{t},$

where $P_{t}$ denotes the product price level, $h_{t}$ denotes labor, $W_{t}$ denotes the nominal wage rate, $a_{t}$ is an exogenous productivity shock, and $F(\cdot)$ is an increasing and concave production function. The optimality condition determining the demand for labor is

$a_{t}F^{\prime}(h_{t})=\frac{W_{t}}{P_{t}},$ (1)

## Page 9

which equates the marginal product of labor to the real wage.

The labor input $h_t$ is assumed to be a composite of a continuum of labor varieties $h_{jt}$ for $j \in [0,1]$. The aggregation technology is of the form

$$
h_t = \left[ \int_0^1 h_{jt}^{1 - \frac{1}{\eta}} dj \right]^{\frac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{\eta}}}, \tag{2}
$$

where $\eta &gt; 0$ is the elasticity of substitution across labor varieties.² The firm chooses the quantity of each labor variety $h_{jt}$ to minimize its total labor cost, $\int_0^1 W_{jt} h_{jt} dj$, subject to the aggregation technology (2), given its desired amount of the labor composite $h_t$ and taking as given the wage of each variety of labor, denoted $W_{jt}$. This cost minimization problem yields the demand for labor of type $j$

$$
h_{jt} = \left(\frac{W_{jt}}{W_t}\right)^{-\eta} h_t, \tag{3}
$$

where

$$
W_t = \left[ \int_0^1 W_{jt}^{1 - \eta} dj \right]^{\frac{1}{1 - \eta}} \tag{4}
$$

is the cost-minimizing price of one unit of aggregate labor, that is, when $h_{jt}$ is chosen optimally for all $j$, the aggregate wage rate $W_t$ satisfies $W_t h_t = \int_0^1 W_{jt} h_{jt} dj$.

## 2.2 Households

The representative household has preferences over streams of consumption, denoted $c_t$, described by the utility function

$$
E_0 \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \beta^t U(c_t), \tag{5}
$$

where $\beta \in (0,1)$ is a subjective discount factor, and $U(\cdot)$ is an increasing and concave period utility function.

The household supplies inelastically $\bar{h}$ units of labor of each variety $j \in [0,1]$.³ The economy faces an exogenous natural rate of unemployment denoted $u_t^n$. The natural rate of unemployment reflects frictions in the labor market unrelated to nominal rigidity (Friedman, 1968). More broadly, we interpret variations in $u_t^n$ as aggregate supply shocks. The effective supply of labor of each variety is then given by $\bar{h}(1 - u_t^n)$. Sometimes the household will not be able to sell all the units of labor it supplies. In these circumstances, employment is demand-determined and the household suffers involuntary unemployment above the natural

### 2.3 Heterogeneous Downward Nominal Wage Rigidity

Each period $t\geq 0$, the nominal wage of every variety $j\in[0,1]$ is assumed to be subject to a lower bound constraint of the form

$W_{jt}\geq\gamma(j)W_{t-1},$ (9)

where $\gamma(j)$ is a positive and increasing function governing the degree of downward nominal wage rigidity of labor variety $j$. The wage lower bound is assumed to depend on the past average wage rate, $W_{t-1}$, instead of on the past variety-specific wage rate, $W_{jt-1}$, to facilitate aggregation.

The rationale for introducing heterogeneity in downward nominal wage rigidity is rooted in empirical and experimental studies documenting two facts: (i) fairness standards play a significant role in shaping downward nominal wage rigidity, and (ii) there is substantial heterogeneity in these standards across workers and sectors. A detailed discussion of this evidence is presented in the literature review in the introduction. Incorporating fairness stan

## Page 11

dards explicitly into the model would be desirable, but lies beyond the scope of this paper. An additional motivation for introducing heterogeneity in downward nominal wage rigidity is empirical evidence indicating that labor productivity varies across firms and workers, and that firms find it easier to cut wages when worker productivity is low *(Fehr and Goette, 2005; Bewley, 1999; Campbell and Kamlani, 1997)*. We develop a model that incorporates this form of heterogeneity in section 5.3.

The function $\gamma(\cdot)$ need not be interpreted as representing a fixed ordering of labor varieties. For example, welders could be represented by $j=0.45$ in period $t$ and by $j=0.73$ in period $t+1$. This could occur, for example, because employment in welding became more regulated. The present formulation of downward nominal wage rigidity nests the homogeneous case studied in *Schmitt-Grohé and Uribe (2016)*, which obtains when the function $\gamma(j)$ is independent of $j$.

The labor market closes with a slackness condition at the level of each labor variety,

$[\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})-h_{jt}][W_{jt}-\gamma(j)W_{t-1}]=0.$ (10)

This condition implies that when an occupation exhibits unemployment above the natural rate, the wage must be at its lower bound. Conversely, if the wage is strictly above the lower bound, the occupation must be at full employment, defined as an unemployment rate equal to the natural rate. Condition (10) has the same logical status as a market clearing condition in a Walrasian setting. Specifically, in a Walrasian environment, the wage $W_{jt}$ would adjust to ensure that the market for each labor variety clears at the desired quantities, $[\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})-h_{jt}]=0$, for all $j$. In the present non-Walrasian environment, however, because of nominal rigidity, $W_{jt}$ might not be able to adjust to ensure market clearing, giving rise to involuntary unemployment.

### 2.4 The Government

The central bank sets the nominal interest rate according to a Taylor rule of the form

$1+i_{t}=\frac{1+\pi^{*}}{\beta}\left(\frac{1+\pi_{t}}{1+\pi^{*}}\right)^{\alpha_{\pi}}\left(\frac{y_{t}}{y}\right)^{\alpha_{y}}\mu_{t},$ (11)

where $\pi^{*}$ denotes the central bank’s inflation target, $y_{t}$ denotes aggregate output, $y$ denotes the steady-state value of $y_{t}$, $\alpha_{\pi}$ and $\alpha_{y}$ are parameters, and $\mu_{t}$ is an exogenous and stochastic monetary shock.

We assume that fiscal policy is passive in the sense that government solvency is satisfied independently of the path of the price level.

2.5 Equilibrium

In equilibrium, aggregate output is given by

$y_{t}=a_{t}F(h_{t}).$ (12)

Market clearing in the goods market requires that consumption equal output,

$c_{t}=y_{t}.$ (13)

We are now ready to define a competitive equilibrium.

###### Definition 1 (Competitive Equilibrium).

A competitive equilibrium is a set of processes $c_{t}$, $y_{t}$, $h_{t}$, $h_{jt}$, $W_{t}$, $W_{jt}$, $P_{t}$, $\pi_{t}$, and $i_{t}$ satisfying (1), (3), (4), and (6)–(13) for all $j\in[0,1]$ and $t\geq 0$, given the initial wage $W_{-1}$ and the exogenous disturbances $a_{t}$, $\mu_{t}$, and $u_{t}^{n}$.

Next, we show that the equilibrium conditions can be written in terms of a single labor variety.

### 2.6 Equilibrium in $j^{*}$ Form

We consider an equilibrium in which for every $t\geq 0$ there exists a cutoff labor variety denoted $j_{t}^{*}\in(0,1)$ that operates at full employment, $h_{j_{t}^{*}t}=\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$, and for which the wage lower bound holds with equality, $W_{j_{t}^{*}t}=\gamma(j_{t}^{*})W_{t-1}$. Evaluating the labor demand (3) at $j=j_{t}^{*}$ yields the condition

$\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})=\left(\frac{\gamma(j_{t}^{*})}{1+\pi_{t}^{W}}\right)^{-\eta}h_{t},$ (14)

where

$\pi_{t}^{W}\equiv\frac{W_{t}}{W_{t-1}}-1$ (15)

denotes wage inflation in period $t$.

All varieties $j<j_{t}^{*}$ also pay the wage $\gamma(j_{t}^{*})W_{t-1}$ and operate at full employment. To see this, suppose first, contrary to the claim, that $W_{jt}<W_{j_{t}^{*}t}$ for some $j<j_{t}^{*}$. Then, by (3) we have that $h_{jt}=(W_{jt}/W_{t})^{-\eta}h_{t}>(W_{j_{t}^{*}t}/W_{t})^{-\eta}h_{t}=\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$, which violates the time constraint (6). Intuitively, since at $W_{j_{t}^{*}t}$ there is full employment, a wage lower than $W_{j_{t}^{*}t}$ would induce a demand for labor in excess of full employment, which is impossible. Suppose now that, contrary to the claim, $W_{jt}>W_{j_{t}^{*}t}$ for some $j<j_{t}^{*}$. Then by the same logic $h_{jt}<\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$. Further, $W_{jt}>W_{j_{t}^{*}t}=\gamma(j_{t}^{*})W_{t-1}>\gamma(j)W_{t-1}$. So we have that in this case $\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})-h_{jt}>0$ and $W_{jt}-\gamma(j)W_{t-1}>0$, which violates the slackness condition (10).

The cutoff labor variety $j_{t}^{*}$ is an important object in this model because it governs the extensive margin of unemployment, that is, how many occupations will operate below potential. Put differently, $j_{t}^{*}$ is a measure of tightness in the labor market: The larger $j_{t}^{*}$ is, the smaller the number of labor varieties suffering Keynesian unemployment will be.

The facts that varieties below $j_{t}^{*}$ are fully employed, $h_{jt} = \bar{h}(1 - u_{t}^{n})$ for $j &lt; j_{t}^{*}$, and that varieties above $j_{t}^{*}$ suffer involuntary unemployment, $h_{jt} &lt; \bar{h}(1 - u_{t}^{n})$ for $j &gt; j_{t}^{*}$, means that for varieties $j &lt; j_{t}^{*}$ employment is supply-determined and that for varieties $j &gt; j_{t}^{*}$ employment is demand-determined. This marks a difference with the new-Keynesian model with Calvo-type nominal wage rigidity in which employment is demand-determined for all labor varieties at all times. This difference arises because, unlike in the Calvo-type wage stickiness model, in the present model workers are not forced to work more hours than they wish at the prevailing wage, which is arguably a more realistic assumption.

## 3 The Wage Phillips Curve

Given the natural rate of unemployment, $u_{t}^{n}$, equations (17) and (18) parametrically represent a contemporaneous relationship involving only unemployment and wage inflation ($u_{t}$ and $\pi_{t}^{W}$). Further, $u_{t}$ and $\pi_{t}^{W}$ are negatively related. To see this, recall that equation (17) implies that $\pi_{t}^{W}$ is increasing in $j_{t}^{*}$ and that equation (18) implies that $u_{t}$ is decreasing in $j_{t}^{*}$. Thus, the model’s implied relationship between unemployment and wage inflation represents a downward sloping wage Phillips curve.

We note that the model implies a contemporaneous wage Phillips curve. In particular, it does not feature future expected inflation. In this sense, the present model departs from the new-Keynesian framework in which the wage Phillips curve is forward looking *(Erceg, Henderson, and Levin, 2000; Galí, 2011)*. In both models, households and firms are rational, optimizing, and forward looking. The reason why the new-Keynesian model produces a forward-looking Phillips curve is its assumption that workers have monopoly power. By contrast, in the heterogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity model proposed here, households and firms are assumed to be price takers in the labor market. In this way, the present model provides microfoundations to Phillips’s original formulation of a contemporaneous wage Phillips curve *(Phillips, 1958)*. The following proposition summarizes this result.

## Page 16

Proposition 1 (The Wage Phillips Curve). The model with heterogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity implies a contemporaneous negative relationship between wage inflation, $\pi_t^W$, and the unemployment rate, $u_t$. This relationship is parametrically defined by equations (17) and (18) and depends on the exogenous supply shock $u_t^n$.

The supply shock $u_t^n$ is therefore a shifter of the wage Phillips curve, whereas all other disturbances, such as monetary shocks, productivity shocks, and preference shocks, cause movements along a given Phillips curve.

## 3.1 Curvature of the Wage Phillips Curve

To illustrate the properties of the wage Phillips curve implied by the model, we consider a linear functional form for $\gamma(j)$ and calibrate the parameters of the model. Specifically, assume that

$$
\gamma(j) = (1 + \pi^*)^\delta(\Gamma_0 + \Gamma_1 j). \tag{20}
$$

This formulation implies that the degree of downward nominal wage rigidity distributes uniformly across varieties, with lower bound $(1 + \pi^*)^\delta \Gamma_0$ and upper bound $(1 + \pi^*)^\delta (\Gamma_0 + \Gamma_1)$. ### 3.2 Shifters of the Wage Phillips Curve

Each data point in Figure 1 should be interpreted, up to possible measurement error, as being associated with a Phillips curve drawn for a different value of the supply shock $u_{t}^{n}$ and possibly other factors determining its position, such as the long-run inflation rate $\pi^{*}$. Figure 2 shows how these shifters affect the position of the wage Phillips curve.

A negative aggregate supply shock in the form of an increase in the natural rate of unemployment, $u_{t}^{n}$, shifts the Phillips curve up and to the right. Intuitively, an exogenous increase in the number of unemployed workers requires more grease in the labor market (i.e., an increase in wage inflation) to maintain a given rate of overall unemployment.

Similarly, an increase in the long-run inflation rate (an increase in $\pi^{*}$) or an increase in the degree of downward nominal wage rigidity (an increase in $\Gamma_{0}$ or $\Gamma_{1}$) shifts the Phillips curve up and to the right. The intuition behind these effects is as follows. Wage inflation acts as a lubricant of the labor market because the higher wage inflation is, the larger the number of activities that are not constrained by the wage lower bound will be. An increase in $\pi^{*}$, $\Gamma_{0}$, or $\Gamma_{1}$ raises the wage lower bound. Thus, the economy needs more lubricant to maintain the same level of unemployment.

An increase in the elasticity of substitution across labor varieties, $\eta$, flattens the wage Phillips curve. Intuitively, the larger is $\eta$, the more sensitive labor demand becomes to changes in the relative wage rate. Thus, an increase in inflation, by reducing the relative wage of constrained labor varieties, causes a larger increase in employment the greater $\eta$ is.

## 4 Nonlinearity: Evidence from U.S. Regional Data

Here, we explore whether the wage Phillips curve features nonlinearities using a panel of data from U.S. Combined Statistical Areas (CSAs). The advantage of employing regional data is that it provides more variation in wage inflation and unemployment than using national data. The use of regional data in the estimation of linear Phillips curves has been adopted by *Fitzgerald and Nicolini (2014)*, *McLeay and Tenreyro (2019)*, and *Hazell et al. (2022)*, among others. Nonlinear regional Phillips curves have been estimated by *Kumar and Orrenius (2016)*, *Leduc and Wilson (2017)*, *Leduc, Marti, and Wilson (2019)*, *Hooper, Mishkin, and Sufi (2020)*, *Cerrato and Gitti (2022)*, and *Gitti (2024)*, among others. Our approach differs from these latter studies in three respects: First, in line with our theoretical model in which workers are assumed not to have market power, our Phillips curve does not include expected inflation. This distinction is relevant for the set of controls that are valid in estimation. Second, also in line with the predictions of the HDNWR model, we estimate a smooth nonlinear wage Phillips curve, as opposed to a piecewise linear specification. And third, our measure of inflation is inflation of hourly wages as measured by the employment cost index (ECI), instead of price inflation, annual wage and salary income per worker, or average hourly earnings. We use an employment cost index based measure of wage inflation, as it mitigates concerns about composition bias.

We estimate four empirical specifications of the regional wage Phillips curve. The dependent variable is the wage inflation rate in region $i$ in quarter $t$, denoted $\pi_{it}^{W}$, expressed in percent per year. All specifications include as dependent variables a constant, the unemployment rate in region $i$ in quarter $t$, $u_{it}$, expressed in percent, the reciprocal of the unemployment rate, $1/u_{it}$, which captures nonlinearity, and an endogenous control variable, denoted $c_{it}$, meant to capture regional supply disturbances. The need to control for supply disturbances arises from the fact that the wage Phillips curve predicted by the HDNWR model contains the supply shock $u_{it}^{n}$. Since this supply shock is a shifter of the wage Phillips

## Page 24

curve—rather than a source of movements along it—failing to control for it would result in the regression error being correlated with unemployment, which would create endogeneity bias in the estimated coefficients. We do not control for expected inflation, as is done in related empirical studies, because the HDNWR model implies a contemporaneous relationship between wage inflation and unemployment.

The first specification we consider includes the right-hand side variables discussed above and a time trend, denoted $t$,

$\pi^{W}_{it}=\alpha_{0}+\alpha_{1}u_{it}+\alpha_{2}u^{-1}_{it}+\alpha_{3}c_{it}+\alpha_{4}t+\epsilon_{it},$ (Model 1)

where $\epsilon_{it}$ is the regression error. The second specification adds regional fixed effects, denoted $\gamma_{i}$,

$\pi^{W}_{it}=\alpha_{0}+\alpha_{1}u_{it}+\alpha_{2}u^{-1}_{it}+\alpha_{3}c_{it}+\alpha_{4}t+\gamma_{i}+\epsilon_{it}.$ (Model 2)

The third specification replaces the time trend $t$ with time fixed effects, denoted $\gamma_{t}$,

$\pi^{W}_{it}=\alpha_{0}+\alpha_{1}u_{it}+\alpha_{2}u^{-1}_{it}+\alpha_{3}c_{it}+\gamma_{t}+\gamma_{i}+\epsilon_{it}.$ (Model 3)

The time fixed effect, $\gamma_{t}$, controls for nationwide shocks, which could be shifters of the regional Phillips curve (nationwide supply shocks) or movements along a given regional Phillips curve (nationwide demand shocks). It follows that adding time fixed effects alleviates the endogeneity bias though at the cost of removing some potentially useful information for identifying $\alpha_{1}$ and $\alpha_{2}$. Following *Leduc and Wilson (2017)*, *McLeay and Tenreyro (2019)*, and *Hazell et al. (2022)*, the fourth, and last, specification replaces the unemployment rate with the unemployment gap. We proxy the unemployment gap by $u_{it}-\bar{u}$, where $\bar{u}$ reflects the steady-state value of the natural rate of unemployment. Consistent with the calibration strategy of the HDNWR model, we proxy $\bar{u}$ by the floor of the minimum unemployment rate in the regional panel, which equals 2 percent ($\bar{u}=2$),

$\pi^{W}_{it}=\alpha_{0}+\alpha_{1}(u_{it}-\bar{u})+\alpha_{2}(u_{it}-\bar{u})^{-1}+\alpha_{3}c_{it}+\gamma_{t}+\gamma_{i}+\epsilon_{it}.$ (Model 4)

All specifications nest a linear Phillips curve when $\alpha_{2}=0$.

The data are taken from the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS). The regions correspond to CSAs. This section characterizes the implied long-run wage Phillips curve and extends the model to allow for endogenous labor supply and cross-sectional heterogeneity in labor productivity.

### 5.1 The Long-Run Wage Phillips Curve

In the long run wage inflation and price inflation are both equal to the long-run inflation rate $\pi^{*}$. Specifically, because consumption is constant in the steady state, the Euler equation (8) implies the long-run Fisher relationship

$i=\frac{1+\pi}{\beta}-1.$

This expression and the Taylor rule (11) imply that in the steady state inflation must be at its target level,

$\pi=\pi^{*}.$

Since in the steady state the real wage is constant, equilibrium condition (19) implies that wage inflation must equal product-price inflation,

$\pi^{W}=\pi^{*}.$

Equilibrium conditions (17) and (18) evaluated at $u_{t}=u$, $\pi_{t}^{W}=\pi^{*}$, and $u_{t}^{n}=u^{n}$ constitute a relationship between inflation and unemployment in the steady state, which we call the long-run wage Phillips curve. It follows immediately that in the absence of wage indexation

### 5.2 The HDNWR Model with Endogenous Labor Supply

Until now, we have considered an economy with an inelastic labor supply. This section shows that the main results are robust to assuming that the labor supply is endogenous.

Specifically, we replace the lifetime utility function (5) with the function

$E_{0}\sum_{t=0}^{\infty}\beta^{t}\left[U(c_{t})-\int_{0}^{1}V(h_{jt}^{s})dj\right],$

where $h_{jt}^{s}$ denotes the amount of labor of type $j$ supplied in period $t$, and $V(\cdot)$ is a convex

## Page 32

labor disutility function. To facilitate aggregation, we use the functional form

$V(h)=\frac{h^{1+\theta}}{1+\theta},$ (23)

which is often used in the related literature *(e.g., Galí, 2015)*. As before, there can be rationing in the labor market: for each labor type $j$, at the going wage $W_{jt}$ households may not be able to sell all the units of labor they offer. The household sets its desired supply of labor of variety $j$ to equate the marginal rate of substitution between labor and consumption to the variety-specific real wage. Formally, the supply of labor of type $j$ is given by

$\frac{V^{\prime}(h_{jt}^{s})}{U^{\prime}(c_{t})}=w_{jt},$ (24)

where $w_{jt}\equiv W_{jt}/P_{t}$. We continue to assume that there is an exogenous amount of involuntary unemployment unrelated to wage stickiness, embodied in the variable $u_{t}^{n}$ denoting the natural rate of unemployment. The restriction that employment, $h_{jt}$, is voluntary now takes the form

$h_{jt}\leq h_{jt}^{s}(1-u_{t}^{n}).$

This expression says that the household is not willing to have more members employed than the ones it voluntarily supplies to the market net of the ones that are naturally unemployed.

The household’s budget constraint and the optimality conditions associated with consumption and bond holdings are unchanged. The firm’s demand for labor of variety $j\in[0,1]$, given by equation (3), is also unchanged.

As before, we consider an equilibrium in which each period $t\geq 0$ there is a cutoff labor variety, $j_{t}^{*}$, that operates at full employment,

$h_{j_{t}^{*}t}=h_{j_{t}^{*}t}^{s}(1-u_{t}^{n}),$ (25)

and for which the wage constraint holds with equality,

$W_{j_{t}^{*}t}=\gamma(j_{t}^{*})W_{t-1}.$ (26)

Combining these two conditions with the labor demand (3) and the labor supply (24) yields

$\frac{V^{\prime}\left(\frac{h_{t}}{1-u_{t}^{n}}\left(\frac{\gamma(j_{t}^{*})}{1+\pi_{t}^{W}}\right)^{-\eta}\right)}{U^{\prime}(c_{t})}=\frac{\gamma(j_{t}^{*})w_{t-1}}{1+\pi_{t}},$ (27)

where

$w_{t}\equiv W_{t}/P_{t}$ denotes the real wage in period $t$. It can be shown that, as in the case of an inelastic labor supply, all labor varieties $j<j_{t}^{*}$ operate at full employment and are paid the same wage as variety $j_{t}^{*}$. Also, all varieties $j>j_{t}^{*}$ are constrained by the wage lower bound and suffer unemployment above the natural rate.

With an endogenous labor supply, the unemployment rate is the ratio of unemployed labor to the total labor supply. Formally,

$u_{t}=\frac{\int_{0}^{1}(h_{jt}^{s}-h_{jt})dj}{\int_{0}^{1}h_{jt}^{s}dj}.$

$u_{t}=u_{t}^{n}+(1-u_{t}^{n})\frac{\int_{j_{t}^{*}}^{1}\left[\left(\frac{\gamma(j)}{\gamma(j_{t}^{*})}\right)^{\frac{1}{\theta}}-\left(\frac{\gamma(j)}{\gamma(j_{t}^{*})}\right)^{-\eta}\right]dj}{j_{t}^{*}+\int_{j_{t}^{*}}^{1}\left(\frac{\gamma(j)}{\gamma(j_{t}^{*})}\right)^{\frac{1}{\theta}}dj}.$ (28)

# 5.3 Heterogeneous Labor Productivity

Thus far we have taken the cross-sectional distribution of the wage lower bound,  $\gamma (j)$ , as given. In this section, we provide a microfoundation. A recent literature has emphasized the role of heterogeneity in labor productivity for macroeconomic adjustment (see, for example, Kaplan, Moll, and Violante, 2018; Bayer, Born, and Luetticke, 2024, and the references cited therein). Motivated by this literature, here we incorporate idiosyncratic productivity shocks into the model and show that this source of heterogeneity can give rise to a convex relationship between wage inflation and unemployment. Because workers suffering negative idiosyncratic productivity shocks require a fall in their real wage to remain fully employed, they are the most likely to be affected by downward nominal wage rigidity. The empirical literature has additionally documented that firms have an easier time cutting wages when worker productivity is low (Fehr and Goette, 2005; Bewley, 1999; and Campbell and Kamlani, 1997). This fact amounts to cross-sectional heterogeneity in the degree of downward nominal wage rigidity. To isolate this effect, we assume that given the level of labor productivity, all workers have the same wage lower bound, but that the wage lower bound is lower for less productive workers. All other aspects of the model are as in the baseline formulation.

## Page 35

The labor input, $h_t$, is assumed to be a composite of efficiency units of labor varieties with the aggregation technology $h_t = \left[ \int_0^1 (z_{jt} h_t(z_{jt}))^{1 - \frac{1}{\eta}} dj \right]^{\frac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{\eta}}}$, where $z_{jt} &gt; 0$ is a stochastic variety-specific level of labor productivity with density function $f(\cdot)$ and $h_t(z_{jt})$ is the level of employment of workers with productivity $z_{jt}$. It is convenient to conduct the analysis in the domain of labor productivities as opposed to in the domain of labor varieties. To this end, we perform a change of variable as follows. Assume without loss of generality that $z_{jt}$ is increasing in $j$. It follows that $j = \mathcal{F}(z_{jt})$, where $\mathcal{F}(\cdot)$ is the cumulative density function associated with the density function $f(\cdot)$. Now introduce the change of variable $z = z_{jt}$. Then we have that $dj = f(z)dz$. Note also that $z \to 0$ as $j \to 0$ and that $z \to \infty$ as $j \to 1$. The aggregation technology can then be written as $h_t = \left[ \int_0^\infty (z h_t(z))^{1 - \frac{1}{\eta}} f(z) dz \right]^{\frac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{\eta}}}$. The firm chooses employment of each variety to maximize profits, $P_t a_t F(h_t) - \int_0^\infty W_t(z) h_t(z) f(z) dz$, taking $P_t$ and $W_t(z)$ as given, where $W_t(z)$ is the nominal wage of labor variety $z$. The firm's cost-minimizing demand for labor with productivity $z$ is

$$
h_t(z) = z^{\eta - 1} \left(\frac{W_t(z)}{W_t}\right)^{-\eta} h_t, \text{ with } W_t = \left[ \int_z \left(\frac{W_t(z)}{z}\right)^{1 - \eta} f(z) dz \right]^{\frac{1}{1 - \eta}}. \tag{29}
$$

When $h_t(z)$ is chosen optimally, the aggregate wage rate $W_t$ satisfies $W_t h_t = \int_z W_t(z) h_t(z) f(z) dz$. The demand for aggregate units of labor, $h_t$, satisfies the optimality condition $P_t a_t F'(h_t) = W_t$.

The household supplies inelastically $\bar{h}$ units of labor of each variety $z$, subject to the constraint $h_t(z) \leq \bar{h} (1 - u_t^n)$. The nominal wage of every variety $z$ is assumed to be subject to a lower bound constraint of the form

$$
W_t(z) \geq z^{\xi} \gamma W_{t-1}. \tag{30}
$$

The parameter $\gamma &gt; 0$ measures the common degree of downward nominal wage rigidity across productivity levels. The parameter $\xi \geq 0$ measures the degree of productivity specific wage rigidity. It aims to capture the empirical regularity that workers experiencing lower productivity are more willing to tolerate wage cuts (Fehr and Goette, 2005). The higher $\xi$ is, the more sensitive the degree of downward wage rigidity to changes in productivity will be. The labor market closes with a slackness condition imposed at the level of each labor variety $z$:

$$
[\bar{h}(1 - u_t^n) - h_t(z)] [W_t(z) - z^{\xi} \gamma W_{t-1}] = 0. \tag{31}
$$

### 5.4 Implications for Wage Change Distributions

There is an empirical literature studying the cross-sectional distribution of year-over-year changes in hourly wages of individual workers using micro data. (See, for example, Daly and Hobijn, 2014; and Jo, 2025.) This literature has documented that the mass of workers experiencing an exact zero change from one year to the next in their nominal hourly wage is cyclical, in particular, it increases with the unemployment rate. This stylized fact is often cited as evidence of downward nominal wage rigidity. So it is natural to be curious about whether the model proposed in the present paper can speak to this stylized fact.

In the heterogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity model, negative demand shocks lead to movements down a given wage Phillips curve. As the economy moves down the wage Phillips curve, $j_{t}^{*}$ falls. Therefore, the mass of constrained workers, $1-j_{t}^{*}$, increases with unemployment. And the larger the negative demand shock is, the larger the mass of constrained workers will be. All workers experiencing a binding wage constraint in consecutive periods, will have the same wage change. To see this, consider a labor variety $j$ that is stuck at the lower bound, so that $W_{jt}=\gamma(j)W_{t-1}$. If the wage constraint is binding for this worker in two consecutive periods, then $W_{jt}/W_{jt-1}$ equals $W_{t-1}/W_{t-2}$. Now, if the economy is in the relatively flat part of the Phillips curve, then the negative demand shock causes a relatively small change in aggregate wage inflation. This has implications for wage changes of individual workers. The model does qualitatively capture the notation that the mass of workers receiving the same wage change goes up in a recession and that this wage change is likely to be small. However, the model falls short of predicting that the mass of workers stuck at their wage lower bound constraint experience an exact zero nominal wage growth, as suggested by the survey-based microdata studies cited above.

6 Regular Dynamics

We have established that the heterogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity (HDNWR) model predicts that the unemployment costs of reducing inflation are much lower at high inflation rates than at low inflation rates. This result concerns the global properties of the model. A natural question is whether for regular fluctuations in a neighborhood around the intended inflation target, the HDNWR model produces equilibrium dynamics that are consistent with conventional intuition. To address this question we compare its predicted dynamics to those induced by the most widely used framework for nominal wage rigidities, namely, the new-Keynesian model with Calvo wage staggering.

We find that for calibrations and shock processes typically considered in the related literature, the predicted dynamics are fairly similar. This result is of interest because unlike the HDNWR model, the new-Keynesian model with Calvo staggering features a forward-looking wage Phillips curve. The claim in this section is not that the similarity in the dynamics predicted by the two models is necessarily valid for all possible calibrations and shock specifications. Rather the claim is that it is valid for conventional ones.

To derive this result the section characterizes the equilibrium dynamics of the HDNWR model and compares them to those implied by a new-Keynesian (NK) model with Calvo-type wage stickiness. The HDNWR model considered here is the one with endogenous labor supply developed in section 5.2.

We have shown that in spite of the fact that the HDNWR model with inelastic or elastic labor supply features occasionally binding constraints at the level of individual varieties of labor, its complete set of equilibrium conditions does not. This means that the model is amenable to a characterization of the equilibrium dynamics using perturbation methods. We summarize this result in the following proposition:

###### Proposition 2 (HDNWR and Perturbation).

The equilibrium dynamics of the HDNWR model with inelastic or elastic labor supply described in Definitions 2 and C1 (Appendix C), respectively, can be approximated using perturbation techniques.

## 7 Conclusion

This paper contributes to understanding nonlinearities in the trade off between wage inflation and unemployment. To this end, it proposes a model with heterogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity. This innovation results in a smooth, convex wage Phillips curve. At high inflation levels, the predicted Phillips curve is relatively steep, indicating that the costs of reducing high inflation can be low in terms of employment. Conversely, at high levels of unemployment, the curve is relatively flat, suggesting that the cost of reducing high unemployment can be low in terms of inflation.

An empirical contribution of the paper is the estimation, using U.S. regional data, of a wage Phillips curve with a smooth nonlinear component that nests as a special case a linear Phillips curve. The result of this estimation suggests that the U.S. wage Phillips curve is significantly nonlinear. The wage Phillips curve predicted by the proposed model captures reasonably well the tradeoff between wage inflation and unemployment implied by this estimate.

Methodologically, the paper contributes to macroeconomic modeling in three ways. First, it shows that market power is not a prerequisite for introducing nominal rigidity in dynamic general equilibrium models. In the proposed model, consumers, workers, and firms are price takers in product and factor markets, and yet nominal prices fail to adjust fast enough to guarantee market clearing at all times.

This result is relevant for modeling nominal rigidity in markets lacking a significant degree of market concentration. In the context of the present paper, it is of use to model nominal wage rigidity in an economy with low levels of labor unionization like the United States. Introducing nominal rigidity without market power implies that the Phillips curve is a contemporaneous relationship between a measure of inflation and a measure of aggregate slack, not mediated by short-run inflationary expectations, much in line with the Phillips curve estimated by Phillips himself.

## Page 43

Second, unlike in the standard new-Keynesian model of nominal wage rigidity, in the model proposed in the present study employment is not demand-determined. That is, workers are not forced to work more hours than they wish at the prevailing wage. We believe that this property makes the theoretical environment more realistic.

Finally, the paper provides a theoretical framework with downward nominal wage rigidity that is amenable to analysis using perturbation techniques. This property arises because, although the model features occasionally binding constraints at the micro level, no such constraints appear at the aggregate level. This is not the case in models with homogeneous downward nominal wage rigidity, which, we suspect, has impeded their adoption for the formulation, computation, and estimation of medium scale models for policy evaluation. In this regard, the proposed framework aims to lower the entry barrier for models with this empirically compelling type of nominal rigidity.

## Appendix A Graphical Determination of Wages and Employment Across Labor Varieties Given $h_{t}$ and $W_{t}/W_{t-1}$

Figure A1 provides a graphical explanation of the determination of wages and employment across labor varieties, given the aggregate variables $h_{t}$ and $W_{t}/W_{t-1}$. The downward sloping curve represents the demand for labor of each variety, $h_{jt}$, as a function of $j$ when the variety-specific wage equals its lower bound, $W_{jt}=\gamma(j)W_{t-1}$. The vertical line represents the labor supply net of natural unemployment, $\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$, as a function of $j$. The intersection of the two lines at point $A$ determines the cutoff variety $j_{t}^{*}$. This is because at point $A$ there is full employment and the wage constraint exactly binds, which are the two conditions defining $j_{t}^{*}$.

Points located to the right of the downward sloping line are infeasible because they imply that $W_{jt}<\gamma(j)W_{t-1}$, which violates the wage lower bound. Points located to the left of the downward sloping line imply that the wage lower bound is slack, $W_{jt}>\gamma(j)W_{t-1}$. Points located to the right of the vertical line are infeasible because they violate the resource constraint $h_{jt}\leq\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$. Points located to the left of the vertical line imply involuntary unemployment, $h_{jt}<\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$. Points to the left of both the downward sloping and the vertical lines are infeasible because they imply that the wage lower bound is slack, $W_{jt}>\gamma(j)W_{t-1}$, and that there is involuntary unemployment, $h_{jt}<\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$, which violates the slackness condition $(W_{jt}-\gamma(j)W_{t-1})(h_{jt}-\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n}))=0$.

Since points located to the right of either curve or to the left of both are infeasible, it follows that in equilibrium pairs $(j,h_{jt})$ must lie on the vertical line if $j<j_{t}^{*}$ and on the downward sloping curve if $j>j_{t}^{*}$. For example, for variety $j^{\prime}<j_{t}^{*}$ in the figure, the equilibrium is at point $B$, where there is full employment and the wage is unconstrained, i.e., workers receive a wage strictly above the lower bound $\gamma(j^{\prime})W_{t-1}$. How much above? If the wage rate of variety $j^{\prime}$ were at its lower bound, $\gamma(j^{\prime})W_{t-1}$, then there would be excess demand given by the distance between points $B$ and $D$. Since wages are upwardly flexible, the wage rate has to increase until the excess demand disappears. It is clear from the figure that this occurs when variety $j^{\prime}$ earns the same wage as variety $j_{t}^{*}$, namely, $\gamma(j_{t}^{*})W_{t-1}$. By contrast, for variety $j^{\prime\prime}>j_{t}^{*}$, the equilibrium is at point $C$, where there is involuntary unemployment and the wage lower bound is binding, i.e., the wage is stuck at its lower bound.

The fact that the equilibrium is on the vertical line for $j<j_{t}^{*}$ and on the downward sloping curve for $j>j_{t}^{*}$ means that for varieties $j<j_{t}^{*}$ employment is supply determined and that for varieties $j>j_{t}^{*}$ employment is demand determined.

# E Pareto Distribution for  $\gamma$

The baseline calibration assumes that the degree of variety-specific downward nominal rigidity is uniformly distributed over the interval  $[(1 + \pi^{*})\Gamma_{0}, (1 + \pi^{*})(\Gamma_{0} + \Gamma_{1})]$ . Here, we consider a case in which the degree of downward nominal wage rigidity has a Pareto distribution, with a minimum value  $\gamma_{m} &gt; 0$  and tail index  $\alpha &gt; 2$ . Specifically, indexing labor varieties by the degree of downward nominal wage rigidity,  $\gamma \in (\gamma_{m}, \infty)$ , we have that the lower bound on

nominal wages now is

$W_{t}(\gamma)\geq(1+\pi^{*})^{\delta}\gamma W_{t-1}.$

The cumulative distribution of $\gamma$, denoted $\mathcal{F}(\gamma)$, takes the form

$\mathcal{F}(\gamma)=1-\left(\frac{\gamma_{m}}{\gamma}\right)^{\alpha}.$

Under this formulation, the three equilibrium conditions that distinguish the DNWRA model from the neo-classical model, namely, equations (14), (17), and (18), become

$\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})=\left(\frac{(1+\pi^{*})^{\delta}\gamma_{t}^{*}}{1+\pi_{t}^{W}}\right)^{-\eta}h_{t},$ (E1)
$(1+\pi_{t}^{W})^{1-\eta}=\left[(1+\pi^{*})^{\delta}\gamma_{t}^{*}\right]^{1-\eta}\left[1-\left(\frac{\gamma_{m}}{\gamma_{t}^{*}}\right)^{\alpha}\left(\frac{\eta-1}{\eta+\alpha-1}\right)\right]$ (E2)

and

$u_{t}=u_{t}^{n}+(1-u_{t}^{n})\left(\frac{\gamma_{m}}{\gamma_{t}^{*}}\right)^{\alpha}\frac{\eta}{\eta+\alpha},$ (E3)

where $\gamma_{t}^{*}$ is the cutoff value of $\gamma$ defined as the labor variety that is fully employed and for which the wage constraint is just binding. $\pi_{t}^{W}=(1+\pi^{*})^{\delta}\left(\frac{1-u_{t}^{n}}{u_{t}-u_{t}^{n}}\frac{\eta}{\eta+\alpha}\right)^{\frac{1}{\alpha}}\gamma_{m}\left[1-\left(\frac{u_{t}-u_{t}^{n}}{1-u_{t}^{n}}\right)\left(\frac{\eta+\alpha}{\eta}\right)\left(\frac{\eta-1}{\eta+\alpha-1}\right)\right]^{\frac{1}{1-\eta}}-1.$ (E4)

It follows immediately that, holding the natural rate of unemployment constant, wage inflation is a decreasing function of unemployment.

### F.1 Parametric Representation of the Wage Phillips Curve

We make the following assumption about the parameter $\xi$:

###### Assumption F1.

The parameter $\xi$ satisfies $\eta(1-\xi)>1$.

This assumption states that wage rigidity cannot increase too quickly as productivity rises.

We consider an equilibrium in which for every $t\geq 0$ there exists a cutoff labor productivity, denoted $z_{t}^{*}$, such that workers with this level of productivity are fully employed,

$h_{t}(z_{t}^{*})=\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$ (F1)

and for which the wage lower bound holds with equality,

$W_{t}(z_{t}^{*})=z_{t}^{*\xi}\gamma W_{t-1}.$ (F2)

This cutoff is of interest because, as in the baseline model, it determines the entire cross-sectional distribution of wages and employment. Claim F1 in section F.2 shows that for any $z>z_{t}^{*}$, there is full employment, $h_{t}(z)=\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$, and the wage rate is unconstrained, $W_{t}(z)>z^{\xi}\gamma W_{t-1}$. Claim F2 in section F.2 shows that for any $z<z_{t}^{*}$, there is involuntary

## Page 56

unemployment, $h_{t}(z)<\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$, and the wage rate is stuck at its lower bound, $W_{t}(z)=z^{\xi}\gamma W_{t-1}$.

Next we analyze the relationship between the cutoff labor productivity level $z_{t}^{*}$ and wage inflation. To this end, following steps similar to those given in the derivation of equation (17) of the baseline model, we write the wage inflation rate as

$(1+\pi_{t}^{W})^{1-\eta}=\int_{z<z_{t}^{*}}\left(\frac{\gamma}{z^{1-\xi}}\right)^{1-\eta}f(z)dz+\int_{z>z_{t}^{*}}\left(\frac{\gamma}{z_{t}^{*}^{1-\xi}}\left(\frac{z}{z_{t}^{*}}\right)^{-\frac{1}{\eta}}\right)^{1-\eta}f(z)dz.$ (F3)

This equation implies that wage inflation and the cutoff productivity level are negatively related. The formal proof is in Claim F3 in section F.2. This result is intuitive: higher inflation reduces the real value of the wage lower bound. In turn this allows the real wage of constrained workers to fall. As a consequence workers with productivity levels below but close to $z_{t}^{*}$, which were previously unemployed, now become fully employed. This means that $z_{t}^{*}$ falls with wage inflation.

The aggregate unemployment rate is defined as

$u_{t}=\int_{z}\frac{\bar{h}-h_{t}(z)}{\bar{h}}f(z)dz.$

As shown above, highly productive workers, namely those with $z>z_{t}^{*}$, are fully employed, $h_{t}(z)=\bar{h}(1-u_{t}^{n})$. Thus the unemployment rate for highly productive workers is $u_{t}^{n}$. Relatively unproductive workers, those with $z<z_{t}^{*}$, suffer involuntary unemployment. Evaluating the labor demand schedule, equation (29), at $z<z_{t}^{*}$ and at $z=z_{t}^{*}$ and taking ratios, we have

$\frac{h_{t}(z)}{h_{t}(z_{t}^{*})}=\left(\frac{z}{z_{t}^{*}}\right)^{\eta(1-\xi)-1},$

which says that the lower productivity is, the lower the employment level will be (recall that by Assumption F1, $\eta(1-\xi)>1$). This is so because, due to downward nominal wage rigidity, the marginal product of labor falls more quickly than the nominal wage. Unemployment can then be written as

$u_{t}=u_{t}^{n}+(1-u_{t}^{n})\int_{z<z_{t}^{*}}\left[1-\left(\frac{z}{z_{t}^{*}}\right)^{\eta(1-\xi)-1}\right]f(z)dz.$ (F4)

According to this expression, the unemployment rate is increasing in the productivity threshold $z_{t}^{*}$. The formal proof is in Claim F4 in section F.2. This is intuitive, because when $z_{t}^{*}$ increases, some workers who were previously fully employed become unemployed.

\frac{dRHS}{dz_t^*} = (\eta - 1) \frac{\frac{\eta(1 - \xi) - 1}{\eta}}{z_t^*} \int_{z &gt; z^*} \left( \frac{\gamma}{z_t^{*1 - \xi}} \left( \frac{z}{z_t^*} \right)^{-\frac{1}{\eta}} \right)^{1 - \eta} f(z) dz &gt; 0,
$$

where the inequality follows from Assumption F1 and the fact that the assumption that $\eta &gt; 0$ together with Assumption F1 imply that $\eta &gt; 1$. The derivative of $LHS$ with respect to $\pi_t^W$ is given by

$$
\frac{dLHS}{d\pi_t^W} = (1 - \eta)(1 + \pi_t^W)^{-\eta} &lt; 0,
$$

where the inequality follows from the fact that the assumption that $\eta &gt; 0$ together with Assumption F1 imply that $\eta &gt; 1$. Thus we have that

$$
\frac{d\pi_t^W}{dz_t^*} = \frac{\frac{dRHS}{dz_t^*}}{\frac{dLHS}{d\pi_t^W}} &lt; 0.
$$

Proof. Differentiating equilibrium condition (F4) with respect to $z_t^*$ yields

$$
\frac{d u_t}{dz_t^*} = (1 - u_t^n) \frac{\eta(1 - \xi) - 1}{z_t^*} \int_{z &lt; z_t^*} \left( \frac{z}{z_t^*} \right)^{\eta(1 - \xi) - 1} f(z) dz &gt; 0,
$$

where the inequality follows from Assumption F1.

G Regular Dynamics: Additional Results

This appendix complements the results of section 6 in two ways. First, it considers a calibration with more wage rigidity, to make it comparable with existing calibrations of the NK model. Second, it compares the equilibrium responses of the HDNWR and NK models to productivity shocks.

### G.1 Higher Wage Stickiness

The calibration of the NK model in section 6 delivers a value of $\theta_{w}$, governing the degree of nominal wage rigidity, of 0.35, which is low relative to those typically used to calibrate NK models. For example, *Galí (2015)* sets $\theta_{w}$ to 0.75. To address this issue, here we also consider a calibration in which $\theta_{w}=1-j^{*}=0.75$. In this case, we recalibrate the parameters $\Gamma_{0}$ and $\Gamma_{1}$ of the function $\gamma(j)$ in the HDNWR model. Specifically, we continue to impose that the steady-state unemployment-inflation pair match its observed median value but drop the requirement that the model match the slope of the Galí-Gambetti wage Phillips curve and instead target a value of 0.75 for $1-j^{*}$. The resulting values of $\Gamma_{0}$ and $\Gamma_{1}$ are 0.9908 and 0.0175.

Figure G1 compares the impulse responses of the HDNWR and NK models when $\theta_{w}=1-j^{*}=0.75$. Because now wages are more rigid than in the case in which $\theta_{w}=1-j^{*}=0.35$ considered in the body of the paper (section 6), the HDNWR and NK models predict a more subdued response of wage inflation and a larger response of unemployment. The important point for the purpose of the present discussion, however, is that both models deliver quite similar dynamics.
