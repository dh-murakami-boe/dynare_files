It's Baaack: The Surge in Inflation in the 2020s and the Return of the Non-Linear Phillips Curve*

Pierpaolo Benigno¹ and Gauti B. Eggertsson²

¹University of Bern
²Brown University and NBER

Abstract

This paper proposes a non-linear New Keynesian Phillips curve to explain the surge in inflation during the 2020s. Economic slack is measured as the ratio of firms' job vacancies to the number of unemployed workers. After presenting empirical evidence of statistically significant nonlinearities, we propose a New Keynesian model with search and matching frictions, complemented by a form of wage rigidity, in the spirit of Phillips (1958), which generates strong nonlinearities. The policy implications include the thesis that appropriate monetary policy can reduce inflation without causing a significant recession and that the recent inflationary surge was primarily driven by a “labor shortage” – that is, an exceptionally tight labor market, which not only triggers inflationary pressures on its own but also amplifies the impact of supply shortages.

# 1 Introduction

This paper proposes a new model of inflation dynamics: the Inverse-L New Keynesian Phillips curve. Named for its distinctive shape – resembling a backward L – the model captures the behavior of inflation under different labor market conditions. The vertical part of the curve represents a tight labor market, where inflation rises steeply, while the flat part represents a looser labor market, where inflation is relatively unresponsive.

We apply this model to analyze the sharp, unexpected rise in U.S. inflation in the early 2020s following the COVID-19 pandemic. Our analysis indicates that both demand and supply shocks contributed to the inflation surge, with demand shocks accounting for approximately two-thirds and supply shocks for one-third. Crucially, when the economy operates beyond the kink of the curve – where the labor market is tight – the nonlinear part of the curve amplifies the effect of supply shocks on inflation. This finding has important implications for monetary policy. Unlike during the Volcker recession that followed the 1970s' Great Inflation, our model suggests the Federal Reserve can achieve a "soft landing" – reducing inflation while causing only a minor increase in unemployment – when operating on the nonlinear part of the Phillips curve.

The U.S. time series data show statistically significant and quantitatively important evidence supporting a nonlinear Phillips curve. Moreover, the data indicate increased amplification of supply shocks

once nonlinearity comes into play. Below, we outline the broader motivation for our approach, the historical background, and the main contribution of this paper.

The early Keynesian literature assumes a stark relationship between prices and output, represented by the inverted (or backward) L shape in the left-hand panel of Figure 1. *Blinder (2022)* labels this as "crude Keynesianism." When output falls below potential, workers sit idle, and factories are partially empty, keeping prices fixed. Once all workers are employed and factories are operating at full capacity, output can no longer increase, and aggregate supply becomes vertical. At this point, the economy enters a neoclassical world of flexible prices. In this region, supply shocks have a disproportionately large effect, partly because labor cannot be substituted due to labor market tightness.

Keynesianism took a sharp turn with *Phillips’ (1958)* seminal work, one of those papers so well-known that they are rarely cited, even in the literature that builds on them. Instead, the author’s name has become synonymous with the "Phillips curve."

The Phillips curve is the best-known and most controversial equation in macroeconomics. Its most common version is:

$\underbrace{\pi_{t}=\kappa x_{t}}_{\text{Keynesian}}$ $+$ $\underbrace{\kappa_{\varrho}\varrho_{t}+\beta E_{t}\pi_{t+1}}_{\text{New Keynesian}}$ (1)
$\text{Phillips Curve}$ $\text{Phillips Curve}$

where $\pi_{t}$ is inflation, $\kappa$ is a coefficient, and $x_{t}$ is some measure of economic activity, such as the output gap, in which case $\kappa>0$, or the unemployment gap, in which case $\kappa<0$. The variable $\varrho_{t}$ corresponds to supply shocks and the coefficient $\kappa_{\varrho}$ measures the impact of supply shocks on inflation, while $\beta$ is the time discount factor, which is between 0 and 1, $E_{t}$ is an expectation operator.

The first part of the equation (1), indicated by the first curly bracket, is the Keynesian Phillips curve famous in the 1960s. *Samuelson and Solow (1960)* suggested that it implied a stable trade-off between inflation and the output gap. The second term, which includes a supply shock and expectations of future inflation, was emphasized by the literature that developed after the 1970s. The formulation in equation (1) is known as the New Keynesian Phillips curve and is currently the backbone of most macroeconomic models.

The 1970s Keynesian Phillips curve collapsed as a stable statistical relationship (see Figure 15 in the Appendix). This empirical failure had a decisive impact on macroeconomics. The rational expectations revolution arguably emerged due to these real-world developments, which made micro-foundations for macroeconomic models mainstream, partially because these foundations explicitly explain how agents form expectations. What made the demise of the Keynesian Phillips curve dramatic – a watershed moment for macroeconomics – is that it was foreseen by *Phelps (1967)* and *Friedman (1968)*. They predicted that a breakdown of this kind *should* occur – as a matter of theory — if

the government tried to exploit the inflation-unemployment trade-off because inflation expectations eventually catch up and endogenously shift the relationship, as shown in equation (1). With inflation rising in the late 1960s, it looked to many observers as if the government accepted excessive inflation in return for higher employment. That the relationship broke down in the 1970s, just as predicted, lent considerable credibility to Friedman and Phelps’s prophecy.

The main explanation for the demise of the Keynesian Phillips curve is the combination of the unanchoring of inflation expectations in the 1970s and supply disruptions. The presence of expected inflation in (1), central to any modern Phillips curve, explains central banks’ focus today on anchoring inflation expectations. As this relationship makes clear, higher inflation expectations have an effect similar to a negative supply shock.

Someone reading Phillips’ classic paper today will realize immediately that modern research pays little or no attention to its central proposition. The Phillips of 1958 would hardly recognize the linear relationship (1) as his construction, even apart from the terms that represent expectations.² First, the relationship Phillips suggests is between unemployment and wage inflation. Second, and more importantly, his point is that this relationship is strongly nonlinear. The curve proposed by Phillips (1958) is

$$
\pi_{t}^{w} = a + b \left(\frac{1}{u_{t}}\right)^{c}
$$

where $\pi_t^w$ is wage inflation, $u_{t}$ is the unemployment rate and $a,b,c$ are estimated coefficients.

The original Phillips curve is plotted in the right-hand panel of Figure 1 using the coefficient values Phillips estimated for the period 1861-1913, with $1 - u_{t}$ plotted on the x-axis representing a higher level of economic activity to relate it to the crude Keynesianism on the left.³ As the figure reveals this relationship is strongly nonlinear and resembles the inverted L of "crude Keynesianism" much more closely than what became synonymous with his name, largely due to when the Phillips curve came to America via Samuelson and Solow (1960), who did not highlight the non-linearity at the core of Phillips’s article.⁴

While Phillips’ paper is empirical, his theoretical argument for the non-linearity of his curve is straightforward. He writes that with “very few unemployed, we should expect employers to bid up wages quite rapidly, each firm and each industry continually tempted to offer a little above the prevailing wage.” But why does the asymmetry arise when unemployment is high? Phillips suggests that “workers are reluctant to offer their services at less than the prevailing rate” so “wages fall only very slowly”.⁵ Phillips’s central proposition and the concluding line of his opening paragraph is a highly nonlinear curve.

The main objective of this article is to resurrect Phillips' original idea to explain the surge in inflation in the 2020s. We first present several suggestive and anecdotal empirical evidence. We then propose a theoretical model for a nonlinear Phillips curve and explore policy implications.

The recent increase in inflation took policymakers by surprise, at least if we go by the Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) of the Federal Reserve. It was also unexpected by private forecasters, as judged by the Survey of Professional Forecasters (SPF), as shown in Figures 16 and 17 in the Appendix. Neither SEP nor SPF anticipated the surge in prices in 2021. And both consistently projected it to decline rapidly to the Fed's 2 percent target rate. Instead, inflation continued to increase in the following quarters.

Three broad observations that motivate our approach largely explain why private forecasters and policymakers were caught flatfooted.

First, the conventional wisdom, forged by the realization of Phelps and Friedman's prophecy in the 1970s, is that persistent increases in inflation are triggered when inflation expectations become unanchored. During the 2020s, however, there was no increase in inflation expectations comparable to that of the 1970s, and both policymakers and market participants accordingly argued that the 2021 surge was transitory.[6] Figure 2 shows inflation expectations according to the Livingstone survey, which asks participants what they expect inflation to be in a year. While this measure peaked at 10 percent in the late 1970s, in the 2020s it rose only modestly. Several alternative measures of expectations that proxy longer-run inflation expectations are even more striking. These measures suggest that the Great Inflation of the 1970s unanchored expectations so drastically that, at their peak, people were

## Page 6

expecting inflation of 10 percent to persist even over a five-to-ten-year horizon. Several such proxies, reported in the Appendix, are used for robustness checks of the empirical analysis in the next section.⁷ In short, one of the key suspects for driving the Great Inflation of the 1970s was simply missing in the 2020s.

A second main reason forecasters missed the surge is that most estimates of the slope of the Phillips curve leading up to the inflation surge, i.e., κ in equation (1), are very low. This view was reflected in virtually all the models used for inflation projections at policy institutions (see, e.g., the discussion in Gopinath (2023) of the IMF model).⁸ According to the widely cited recent estimate of Hazell, Herreno, Nakamura and Steinsson (2022), for example, a 1-percentage-point reduction in unemployment generates only a 0.34-point increase in inflation. Their analysis uses a carefully designed identification strategy based on a cross-section of U.S. states from 1978-2018. While several commentators suggested that demand was considerably above potential output, mainstream macroeconomic models predicted that this should have a modest inflationary impact.⁹

Finally, a third culprit in the 1970s was supply disruptions. A key difficulty in explaining the inflation of the 2020s by supply disruptions alone is that conventional measures which researchers used before the inflation surge, such as the difference between headline and core inflation or the difference between the rates of change in the import-price and GDP deflators, did not increase nearly as much in the 2020s as in the 1970s, and were generally not quantitatively out of line with supply disruptions observed since the 1970s, as we show in Figure 20 in the Appendix.

On the surface, then, supply disruptions seem like a less plausible candidate for the inflation surge in the 2020s relative to the 1970s, especially as inflation expectations have remained anchored, and as long as one only considers supply measures which active researchers considered relevant before the surge, rather than measures researchers have used to rationalize the inflation surge ex-post.¹⁰ As we will see, however, even if we consider supply disturbances measured conventionally, they can have a major impact when interacted with a tight labor market, causing the economy to enter the nonlinear part of the Phillips curve, leading supply shocks to become supercharged. In addition, our statistical analysis suggest a large unexpecialized spike in unexplained inflation using traditional methods at the very beginning of the surge, that can plausibly be attributed to temporary supply side bottle necks.

This brings us to the labor market. A key statistic in both our empirical analysis and modeling framework is labor market tightness, captured by $\theta=\frac{\text{Job Vacancies}}{\text{Unemployed workers}}$, which is a standard measure of tightness in the search and matching literature (for a recent contribution in this literature, see, e.g., Michaillat and Saez, 2023, who argue that the efficient value of $\theta$ is unitary, corresponding to the Beveridge criterion of full employment). The numerator, i.e., the number of vacancies at firms, summarizes how many jobs firms seek to fill, while the denominator represents the number of job seekers.

Consider the following question: Over the past 115 years in U.S. history, there have been six major inflation surges: WWI, WWII, the Korean War, the Vietnam War, the Great Inflation of the 1970s, and the recent inflation surge. What do they have in common? Figure 3, for the sample 1913 Q1 – 1959 Q4, and Figure 4, for the sample 1960 Q1 – 2024 Q2, provide an answer by plotting labor tightness in the upper panel and inflation in the lower panel. We see that in all inflation surges but one (the 1970s), labor tightness crosses 1. In the 1960-2024 period, the average value of $\theta$ is 0.56. This average is relatively close to our empirical assessment of the state of the labor market as neither inflationary nor deflationary.

The exact value of $\theta^{*}$, the threshold for non-linearity, is an empirical question we will discuss. We find that $\theta^{*}=1$ is a reasonable approximation based on empirical criteria discussed in the next section. Our theoretical analysis suggests that it should vary over time, and our result may reflect a lack of statistical power. We regard its exact value across countries and time as an open question. The definition of a labor shortage through $\theta>1$ is intuitive, as it means that there are more jobs firms are looking to fill than workers are looking for. This is how Beveridge, the founding father of labor market literature, defined full employment– the level above which the labor market becomes tight– which we term the "Beveridge threshold." A value of $\theta=1$ is also the criterion Michaillat and Saez (2023) argue indicates when the labor market achieves efficiency, much in the spirit of Beveridge full employment criteria.

Focusing on the last 63 years, situations of labor shortage emerged on two occasions: during the Vietnam War in the late 1960s, a period also marked by a substantial demand stimulus due to tax cuts engineered by President Johnson, as documented by Blinder (2022), and the period around the COVID-19 epidemic. Both periods lie outside the typical econometric estimates window of the slope of the Phillips curve. However, the two periods when the labor market crossed the Beveridge threshold also correspond to inflation surges, as shown by the shaded region in the bottom plot of Figure 4. In other words, labor shortages go hand in hand with inflationary surges. Yet, since labor shortages are rare, our findings are consistent, for example, with Hazell et al. (2022) and several other studies that focus on sub-periods when there are no labor shortages in the sample and the Phillips curve is very flat, which is also what we find.

If this claim is taken at face value, then the same figure should depict aggregate demand instead of supply, since supply shocks are moving equilibrium along aggregate demand. Yet, it remains a challenge for proponents of the cost-push interpretation to explain the pattern of inflation and tightness shown in the picture as aggregate demand in a model. The objective of this paper is to provide a proper econometric identification of aggregate supply in the data shown in the figure and to propose a theoretical model – an Inv-L New Keynesian Phillips curve – that can be consistent with this interpretation.

The first main contribution of this paper is empirical. In line with the earlier literature, we add more structure to the data by considering a series of regressions with both fixed and time-varying coefficients, adding controls, creating proxies for expectations, using an instrumental variable approach, and suggesting two alternative ways of testing non-linearity.

The empirical analysis leads to two major findings. First, there is evidence in favor of substantive non-linearity in the Phillips curve, which is estimated to be statistically significant. This result aligns with the visual impression given by Figure 5: in periods of labor shortage, labor market tightness has a much more significant impact on inflation. Second, supply shocks have a larger effect when there is a labor shortage, and this increase is statistically significant.

The second main contribution of the paper is to resurrect Phillips’ original idea of an inverted-L curve, as shown in Figure 1, within the canonical New Keynesian framework. We believe retaining the core elements of the New Keynesian framework, as laid out in *Clarida, Galí, Gertler (1999)*, *Woodford (2003)* and *Galí (2016)*, is essential for it highlights the role of anchoring inflation expectations which we find to be essential. Our objective is to develop a theory that accounts for both the Great Inflation of the 1970s (when the labor market was slack but inflation expectations were unanchored) and the inflation surge of the 2020s (when the labor market was tight but inflation expectations were firmly anchored). Accounting for both episodes is necessary if one hopes to use the model to interpret the time series data and obtain statistically significant results.

While the elements of our model that generate inflation expectations in the Phillips curve are standard, drawn from the New Keynesian framework, non-linearities in the Phillips curve arise due to an imperfect labor market. This is modeled via a standard search and matching function. In the spirit of Phillips, there are non-linearities in how wages are set. While firms are tempted to outbid one another during a labor shortage, and workers are happy to accept higher wages, the margin for cutting wages is limited during high unemployment as argued by *Phillips (1958)*. This is because workers refuse to accept jobs that pay much below the prevailing wage despite elevated levels of unemployment. Our assumption relies on the extensive empirical literature documenting that wages are downward

We derive a Phillips curve of the same form as (1) with three major differences. First, instead of the output or unemployment gap, the explanatory variable, $x_t$ in equation (1), is labor market tightness, measured by the log deviation of $\theta$ from its steady state. Second, when there is a labor shortage, i.e., $\theta_t > \theta_t^*$, the slope $\kappa$ changes and becomes steeper than usual. This captures the non-linearity that Phillips emphasized and arises due to exactly the same economic forces he proposed. Third, when there is a tight labor market, supply shocks are transmitted to inflation with greater force. Meanwhile, inflation expectations play a major role in either regime, and their relative strength depends on the details of the model specification.

A common objection to the idea that labor market tightness triggered the inflation surge is that some measures of wages fell during the initial phase of the surge. The standard New Keynesian model uses average wages or labor share as proxies for real wages.¹⁵ Both measures fell during the initial phase of the surge because many wage contracts are fixed in nominal terms, so that real wages did not keep up with inflation.

A central point of our theoretical model is that average wage costs (or labor share) do not accurately reflect firms' marginal costs during a labor shortage. Instead, we argue that the cost of hiring new workers is a more relevant measure of marginal costs in such times, with the gap between these two measures widening substantially. When a firm needs to increase production, it typically relies on existing workers, making the primary cost of expanding output the cost of attracting new workers. Drawing on both statistical and anecdotal evidence, we show that the cost of hiring new workers rises rapidly at the onset of an inflation surge.

The most prominent paper arguing that wages played a limited role in explaining the initial inflation surge is an influential study by Bernanke and Blanchard (2024). In their framework, however, labor market tightness affects inflation solely through its impact on average wages, measured by the Employment Cost Index (ECI). Since ECI actually fell during the early phase of the surge, their conclusion follows from the assumption that average wages serve as a proxy for marginal costs, giving supply shocks a much more prominent role. Their findings indicate that supply shocks had a substantial impact at the start of the inflationary surge, while labor market tightness played a smaller role. Although we do not wish to overstate the differences between our findings, our model does share many essential elements with theirs. Our empirical results align broadly as the inflation surge progresses. However, one key distinction is that our empirical strategy allows for supply shocks to amplify inflationary pressures in the presence of a labor shortage—a feature that emerges naturally from our theoretical framework.

Our work relates to several strands of the literature. We contribute to labor market models in the spirit of Mortensen and Pissarides’ (1994) search and matching framework, among others. We introduce employment agencies that optimally determine how many vacancies to post, which pins down wages, thereby resolving the classic problem of wage indeterminacy (see, for example, Hall, 2005). Additionally, we impose a constraint on the employment-agency optimization problem by introducing a wage norm that adjusts only gradually under normal circumstances, yet anchors to the wages that would prevail under a flexible wage-setting regime.

We cast our model in a New Keynesian framework with price rigidities, thus presenting a theory of the relationship between unemployment and monetary policy alternative to influential papers by Blanchard and Galí (2010a), Galí (2009) and Michaillat (2014) among others. We also contribute to the extensive literature following Phillips (1958), which formalizes the relationship between inflation and various measures of economic activity. Our contribution is to derive the Inv-L NK Phillips curve when the measure of economic slack is given by the vacancy-to-unemployed ratio combined with assumptions about wage setting that give rise to non-linearities.

The idea that Blinder (2022) labels crude Keynesianism, depicted in Figure 1, is closely related to Friedman’s (1964, 1993) "plucking model," according to which "output is viewed as bumping along the ceiling of maximum feasible output now and then." This is also the implication of the crude Keynesian view and corresponds closely to the non-linearity in the original Phillips curve. Dupraz, Nakamura and Steinsson (2019) have recently resurrected Friedman’s idea of a plucking model, both with evidence and a formal theoretical framework. Their theoretical framework, like us, rely on downward rigid wages. The idea of an inverse L-shaped supply function is also found in much of the literature on secular stagnation, such as Eggertsson, Mehrotra and Robbins (2019) using similar modeling devices as well as works by Benigno and Ricci (2011) and Schmitt-Grohe and Uribe (2016) emphasizing downward rigid wages. Our theoretical contribution relative to this works is twofold. First, we incorporate an inverse-L aggregate supply mechanism into an otherwise standard New Keynesian model with a forward-looking price setting. The forward-looking price setting is critical to account for both the inflation surge of the 2020s and that of the 1970s. Second, we explicitly integrate $v/u$ into the model, which has been found to be a more empirically relevant measure of economic slack in recent years than unemployment as we discuss in the next section.

Recent works by Abo-Zeid and Sheng (2024) and Michaillat and Saez (2024a) have also provided labor market models in which the ratio $v/u$ plays a crucial role in explaining inflation dynamics. Abo-Zeid and Sheng (2024) additionally incorporate downward nominal wage rigidities to account for the sluggish behavior of wages during the pandemic. Michaillat and Saez (2024a) present a model in which inflation is on target when the ratio $v/u$ equals the critical unitary value. Therefore, inflation picks up for values of the ratio larger than that critical value unlike in our setting where the inflation target is met for $\theta<1$.

A portion of the literature seeks to address whether supply shocks or demand shocks were the primary driver of the inflation surge. One interpretation of our work is that it reconciles the findings reported in these studies. Demand shocks are a plausible explanation for why the labor market became so tight, even though our model also suggests that a decline in labor force participation may have contributed. However, our estimation indicates that supply shocks also played an important role, even though our measures remains within historical norms. This is because, according to both our empirical analysis and theoretical model, supply shocks have a much greater impact in a labor market shortage.

Given the limited sample size of time-series data, cross-sectional data analysis is increasingly employed. Cerrato and Gitti (2023) conduct a new study in the spirit of Hazzell et al. They find that the slope of the Phillips curve tripled in the aftermath of the pandemic. Overall, we interpret these findings as broadly aligned with the evidence we present.

2 Empirical Results

Our strategy is to stay as close as possible to the existing literature on Phillips curve estimation, which has a long history and is briefly reviewed below. Our innovation is simple: we introduce a dummy variable that interacts with supply disturbances and labor market tightness. The model in Section 3 directly implies these extensions.

Although Figure 5 hints at the possibility of a nonlinear Phillips curve, each point on a scatter plot represents an equilibrium outcome viewed through the prism of a general equilibrium model. In the model we present, for instance, each data point is determined by the intersection of the Phillips Curve (aggregate supply) and the aggregate demand. Aggregate demand is influenced by the spending decisions of households and the government, as well as by monetary policy. Consequently, from a theoretical perspective, one may not have strong reasons to believe that the scatter plot, viewed in isolation, provides compelling evidence one way or the other.

The identification problem inherent in a scatter plot of this kind has been well understood for over a century, dating back at least to *Lenoir (1913)*. If only the supply curve shifts, the data traces out demand. Conversely, if only demand shifts, the data traces out aggregate supply. Viewed in this light, the data for the 1960s, displayed in the upper-left panel of Figure 5, is particularly interesting. According to the measures discussed below, the factors influencing the Phillips curve’s shift (such as various proxies for inflation expectations and supply disturbances) remained relatively stable during this period. Meanwhile, shifts in aggregate demand during the 1960s are well documented *(see, for example, Blinder, 2022)*. The figure speaks for itself.

The literature on Phillips curve estimation and the conditions under which it is identified is extensive. We do not attempt to survey it here. *McLeay and Tenreyro (2019)* provide lucid account that highlights various challenges to identification and possible solutions. For our purposes, an important takeaway (see Section 5 of their paper) is that if supply shocks and inflation expectations are adequately controlled for, the Phillips curve can be empirically recovered.

Our preferred benchmarks consist of two empirical specifications. The first is an ordinary least-squares regression with constant coefficients, allowing for piecewise nonlinearity through a dummy variable. The second benchmark specification, which is discussed in Appendix B, permits time variation in all coefficients and is estimated using a Kalman filter. In summary, our main conclusion is that we find statistically significant nonlinearity in the Phillips curve under both specification when there is a labor shortage i.e. $\theta>\theta^{*}$. Furthermore, the benchmark specification we present is robust across various alternatives summarized in Section 2.4 and detailed in the Appendix. Before proceeding, let us clarify why we adopt the strategy of the recent literature cited in the introduction that emphasizes $v/u$ instead of unemployment rate alone.

2.1 Why is it useful to use vacancy-to-unemployed ratio as measure of economic slack?

The inflation surge in the 2020s began in March 2021 as the U.S. economy was recovering from the recession triggered by COVID-19. During this period, both the core Personal Consumption Expenditures (PCE) index and the Consumer Price Index (CPI) exceeded the Federal Reserve’s 2 percent target for year-on-year inflation. The core PCE and CPI peaked at 6 percent and 6.6 percent, respectively, in September 2022, while the Federal Reserve began increasing the Federal Funds rate in March 2022.

One reason the Federal Reserve delayed rate increases for a full year after inflation exceeded its target was that traditional measures of slack, such as unemployment, remained well above pre-pandemic levels. For instance, in March 2021, the unemployment rate stood at 6 percent, while the Federal Reserve Open Market Committee considered maximum employment to be 4 percent. Another widely used metric, the prime-age-to-population ratio, was 76.9 in March 2021, considerably below the pre-pandemic ratio of 80.5.

It wasn’t until these metrics returned to their pre-pandemic levels in March 2022 that the Federal Reserve began raising rates. [20]

However, despite traditional measures of slack indicating little reason for inflationary pressures from the labor market, the ratio of vacancy to unemployed was flashing red. Many firms were desperately seeking to hire more workers, with numerous establishments partially closing and displaying signs using "labor shortage" as reason for the temporary closure (see for example anecdotal evidence from the U.S. in Figure (19)) . The vacancy-to-unemployed ratio exceeded 1 in May 2021, reaching its peak in March 2022 when the Federal Reserve initiated rate hikes. By that time, the v/u ratio had surpassed 2, signifying the tightest labor market since World War II. It is due to the signal of tight labor market embedded in v/u during the recent inflationary surge that a number of recent empirical studies have opted to use it instead of more traditional measures of labor tightness, such as unemployment, to account for the rise in inflation. [21] As illustrated in Figure 23 in the Appendix, in the decade leading up to the inflation surge, the v/u ratio essentially provided the same information as unemployment alone. It was only during the inflation surge in the 2020s that the vacancy-to-unemployed ratio began to convey fundamentally different information compared to unemployment rate alone. [22]

### 2.3 Robustness to alternative measures of $\theta^{*}$ and supply shocks

It is widely recognized that Phillips curve estimates are highly sensitive to the specific empirical specification and the choice of variables. For a recent survey, see *Mavroeidis, Plagborg-Moller, and Stock (2014)*. While conducting a comprehensive exploration of this sensitivity is beyond the scope of this paper, we present the precise specification for each variable in Table 1 and demonstrate how alternative assumptions can influence the results. In essence, while the coefficients’ magnitudes are sensitive to the exact specification, in line with existing literature, our two key empirical findings remain robust: i) there is statistically significant evidence suggesting that the Phillips curve’s slope increases when $\theta>\theta^{*}$, and, under the same conditions, ii) supply shocks have a more substantial impact on inflation.

Our selection of $\theta^{*}=1$ was primarily motivated by its compelling theoretical interpretation, as discussed in the introduction. This choice was also supported by the observation that readers can verify from Figure 5, which suggests that $\theta^{*}=1$ serves as a reasonable approximation. To formally justify this choice, we take an agnostic approach about the threshold and select it by maximizing the likelihood function across piecewise linear models with varying threshold values. Using the number obtained by this procedure we replicate the results presented in Table 1. The outcomes are summarized in Table 2 in Appendix A with all other tables. The results for the latter sample are essentially the same. However, the results for the entire sample indicate that the additional effect of the supply shock when $\theta>\theta^{*}$ is statistically significant.

As illustrated in Figure 24, the likelihood function across different values for the threshold exhibits a relatively flat profile between the optimal choice of $\theta^{*}$ and 1, especially for the sample period 2008-2024, in which the optimal threshold is 0.9743. This is one of the reasons we chose to use 1 as the benchmark, given its historical usage in the literature and its appealing theoretical interpretation. As we will see in the next section, however, our theory does not make a strong prediction about the specific value of $\theta^{*}$. It may vary across regions, countries and over time. We leave further exploration of this topic for future research.

2.6 Relation to the literature finding small role for labor market tightness and the importance of new wages

Some recent studies, such as Bernanke and Blanchard (2024) and Beaudry, Hou, and Portier (2024), argue that the slope of the Phillips curve has not significantly changed and instead attribute most of the inflation surge to supply shocks and potentially inadequate responses by the Federal Reserve to these shocks. We will defer a comprehensive discussion of why we believe our interpretation of the data, which suggests a non-linear Phillips curve, is more consistent with the observed outcomes until later in the paper, as this is best explained once our theoretical framework has been outlined.

However, before proceeding, we would like to highlight a central aspect of the data that has in our view received insufficient attention. A core feature of the standard New Keynesian model is that the marginal costs of production can be captured by the aggregate labor share, often approximated by metrics such as the employment cost index (ECI). This index reflects average labor costs. Since the labor share actually declined during the inflation surge and average employment costs did not rise substantially in the initial stages, many observers concluded that the tight labor market was not responsible for the surge.

An important observation, however, is that the rate of wage growth for newly hired workers was substantially higher during the inflation surge than suggested by average wages. This is illustrated in Figure 27, which compares wage growth for job switchers over the past 25 years. As the Figure shows, the rate of wage increases for job switchers was significantly higher than for those who remained in their jobs. One aspect of this data is that some of the wage growth among job switchers may be due to improved match quality between employers and new hires.

An alternative approach is to consider the wage growth of posted wages for the same job vacancy, thus somewhat mitigating the bias associated with improvement in match quality of people that switch occupation. This is explored by Crump et al. (2024), and is also shown in Figure 28, which tracks the evolution of wages posted for specific jobs. As the figure clearly shows, wage growth for unfilled jobs rose at a very high rate, particularly for lower-income positions.

This observation serves as an important motivation for our modeling approach, where newly negotiated wages, rather than average wages, are the relevant factor for marginal costs. Moreover, we will demonstrate that the vacancy-to-unemployment ratio, $v/u$, is a sufficient statistic for new wages in the context of a labor shortage. This implies that, unlike in Bernanke and Blanchard (2024), $v/u$ can have a direct impact on inflation without needing to operate through some measure of average wages or labor share, which we already knew from observed data fell at the beginning of the surge due to unexpected inflation and rigidities in the wage setting process.

# 3 The model

Like the canonical New Keynesian model, ours is designed to capture the forward-looking behavior of firms, which gives rise to expectations about future inflation, as shown in equation (1). The main distinction in our model, compared to the standard New Keynesian framework, lies in the labor market, which introduces non-linearities. This new feature not only increases the sensitivity of inflation to labor market tightness but also amplifies the impact of supply shocks.

The labor market is modeled via search and matching. As is well known there is a variety of ways of modeling the real wage rate in this class of models, since each employment match generates a "surplus" shared between firms and workers. Here we propose a wage-setting mechanism that is motivated by the observation of Phillips (1958), that wages appear to be highly flexible when the market is tight and fall only slowly under regular circumstances.

The key result is that $\kappa^{tight} > \kappa$, as shown in Figure 8. At a given level of tightness, denoted by the vertical line, a tighter labor market increases inflation above the $2\%$ target. However, the curve is clearly quite flat. Once $\theta$ reaches $\theta^{*} = 1$ (or $\ln \theta = 0$), inflation is barely above target at $2.4\%$. It is only when $\theta$ crosses $\theta = 1$ and enters a regime of labor shortage that it exerts significant inflationary pressure. This is what we term the Beveridge threshold in follow-up work, see Benigno and Eggertsson (2024). Increasing $\theta$ from 0.4693, which is the estimate for labor tightness being neither inflationary nor deflationary, to 1 only increases inflation from $2\%$ to $2.4\%$. But, in labor shortage territory, raising $\theta$ from 1 to 2 increases inflation from $2.4\%$ to $6\%$. In our framework, the economy typically operates below the Beveridge threshold, beyond which wages become flexible. Accordingly, under normal circumstances, wages remain sluggish but are gradually pulled toward the flexible wage level.

When there is labor shortage, the response of inflation to supply shocks, $\phi_t$, is also more pronounced, i.e. $\kappa_{\varrho}^{tight} > \kappa_{\rho}$. The effect of inflation expectations, however, is not clear-cut. It depends on how wages react to expectations of future inflation. As we will see, there are some parameter values that capture the idea of a wage-price spiral, in which case, in a slack labor market, inflation is even more responsive to expectations of future inflation than during labor shortage, i.e. $\kappa_{\beta} > \beta$.

Section 4 summarizes the microfoundations of equation (4). Section 5 discusses policy implications where the model is cast in a form familiar to most readers.

## 3.1 Households

There is a continuum of representative households of measure one. The members have different disutilities of working. No decision is made about each member's hours of work (intensive margin), but the household does decide how many members work (extensive margin). In other words, the household $j$ chooses the labor market participation rate. ### 3.3 Wage determination

The central idea in *Phillips (1958)* is that the relationship between nominal wage growth and labor market tightness is non-linear. He argues that even if demand for labor is low workers are unwilling to take jobs at wages below the "prevailing rates." This implies that despite low demand for workers, and high unemployment "wage rates fall only very slowly." Yet the converse is not true according to Phillips. Workers are perfectly happy to accept wages that are *higher* than the prevailing wage rate. For this reason when labor markets are sufficiently tight "we should expect employers to bid wage

## Page 32

rates up quite rapidly.” To capture Phillips idea we assume that the wage of a worker hired at time $t$ is:

$W_{t}^{new}=\max\{W_{t}^{ex},P_{t}w_{t}^{flex}\}$ (27)

where $w_{t}^{flex}$ is the flexible real wage, i.e. the wage rate that clears the market in the absence of any constraints, while $W_{t}^{ex}$ is wages of workers that are attached to existing firm relationships. To see how this captures Phillips idea, consider the possibility that the demand for labor is weak. In this case, as we will see shortly, then the prevailing wage rate, i.e. $W_{t}^{ex}$ , is greater than the wage rate that would clear the market, i.e. $P_{t}w_{t}^{flex}$. The max operator says that newly hired workers are unwilling to work for a lower wage rate than what is prevailing at the firm, so that if $W_{t}^{ex}>P_{t}w_{t}^{flex}$ then $W_{t}^{new}=W_{t}^{ex}$. Yet, since workers are perfectly happy to work for higher wages than the existing workforce, then if the labor market is sufficiently tight, and $W_{t}^{ex}<P_{t}w_{t}^{flex}$, we have that $W_{t}^{new}=P_{t}w_{t}^{flex}$. We can restate equation (27) in real terms

$w_{t}^{new}=\max\{w_{t}^{ex},w_{t}^{flex}\}.$ (28)

As is well known in search and matching literature the wage rate is not in general determined, since each employment relationship generates a surplus. How the surplus is divided between the employer and firms can be done in several different ways, the most common in the labor-search literature is Nash bargaining. Models incorporating price stickiness typically assume that the real wage is exogenous. One of the theoretical contributions of this paper is to move away from these assumptions in favor of Phillips idea of asymmetric response of wages to tightness and slack.

To do this, we propose a simple model of employment agencies, who have access to the matching technology, and screen workers to be eligible for employment. This will provide the foundation for the flexible wage rate. Once we derive the flexible wage rate, we show how existing wage rates evolve as a function of the flexible wage rate. There is a good reason to start with the flexible wage for it will serve as an anchor for the wages of existing workers. We will assume, as observed in the data, that when the wages of new employees increase, then wages of existing employees increase as well, but to a lesser extent.

#### 3.4.1 Flexible wages

There is a continuum of employment agencies of measure one. The employment agencies find workers suitable for employment by firms. Since each agency is small, they take as given the wage rate and the rate of matches per vacancy posted. When wages adjust, the number of matches per vacancies posted is:

$n(\theta_{t})=\frac{M_{t}}{V_{t}}=\frac{m_{t}(U_{t})^{\eta}(V_{t})^{1-\eta}}{V_{t}}=m_{t}(\theta_{t})^{-\eta}.$ (30)

It charges a one time fee $\gamma_{t}^{b}$ to the firm that is proportional to the salary of a new worker it screens for employment, while incurring a cost $\gamma_{t}^{c}$ for every vacancy it posts. The number of matches it generates is then given by $n(\theta_{t})V_{t}(j)=m_{t}(\theta_{t})^{-\eta}V_{t}(j)$. The revenues and the cost of the employment agency are measured in units of the final good. Profits are:

$\underbrace{\gamma_{t}^{b}w_{t}^{flex}m_{t}\theta_{t}^{-\eta}}_{\text{Marginal benefit}}V_{t}(j)-\underbrace{\gamma_{t}^{c}}_{\text{Marginal cost}}V_{t}(j)$ (31)

$\underbrace{\gamma_{t}^{b}w_{t}^{flex}m_{t}\theta_{t}^{-\eta}}_{\text{Marginal benefit}}=\underbrace{\gamma_{t}^{c}}_{\text{Marginal cost}},$ (32)

## 3.4.3 Endogenizing the wage norm

The key idea introduced by Phillips is that while new workers are reluctant to accept wages below the existing levels, they are quite willing to accept higher wages, leading to a rapid acceleration in wages in a very tight labor market. But what can we reasonably assume about the behavior of existing workers? There is considerable evidence suggesting that the wages of existing workers are downward rigid. However, even with this rigidity, some degree of adjustment is still observed.

In this analysis, we adopt a pragmatic approach, assuming that wages of existing workers evolve according to a relatively flexible specification. This approach nests the fully flexible wage at one extreme and the exogenous wage, as commonly seen in the existing literature just reviewed, at the other. Specifically, we model the wage evolution as follows:

$$

$$

# 4 The Inv-L NK Phillips Curve

The model we have just sketched out can be presented closer to the spirit of Phillips' original suggestion (depicted in Figure 1) than later incarnations of his work. The central result is that this modified curve now takes an inverse-L shape, as in Figure 1, in a first order approximation around the steady state. Two main economic propositions underlie the inverse-L shape. The first is simply that while, given sufficient time and price incentives, most factors of production can typically be increased in one way or another, one factor will always be in limited supply over any reasonable time horizon: the number of people who can work. Second, it has long been recognized that more than other prices the price of labor (wages) falls slower even when it is in excess supply (high unemployment). Together, these observations imply that over some range, an increase in demand increases production without significant inflation pressures, as more people are drawn into the labor force and are gainfully employed. Firms increase their production while their marginal costs only modestly increase. However, given the first proposition, this process is bound to hit a wall once the labor force is fully employed. Then higher demand expresses itself mostly in higher inflation.

## 5 The policy framework for the Inv-L NK Phillips Curve

We have demonstrated that the Phillips curve can be expressed in terms of $\hat{\theta}_{t}$, aligning the model’s predictions with our findings and recent empirical work, which indicates that this measure of economic tightness is a better predictor of inflation than other common proxies of aggregate demand. Additionally, there is a direct connection between $\theta$ and output. Since most policy discussions revolve around output and inflation, it is advantageous to cast the model in these more conventional terms, which we do here. Our objective is to show that our general framework can be applied to explain three significant episodes central to U.S. monetary policy analysis: the inflation surge of the 2020s, the "missing disinflation" following the financial crisis of 2008, and the Great Inflation of the 1970s. We utilize the results from our regression analysis to parameterize the model, thereby evaluating the extent to which it can rationalize, at least in broad terms, the observed patterns in U.S. data.

An important corollary of the AS curve being steep is that it requires a much smaller drop in output to bring down inflation. Compare again the equilibrium points E’ and E” in the case where the Phillips curve is flat. In the former case, bringing down inflation from 6.2% to 2% reduces output by about 2.4%. In comparison, bringing down inflation from E” implies a reduction of 8.5%.

#### 5.1.1 Understanding the soft landing, the Beveridge curve and the informational content of $\theta_{t}$

The reduction of the inflation spike from 2022 Q2 to the end of our sample in 2024 Q2 was accomplished without any increase in unemployment or reduction in output. While considering that the AS curve is steeper when $\hat{Y}_{t}>\hat{Y}^{*}$ rationalizes why there was not a significant reduction in output compared to assuming a flat Phillips curve, there are two additional forces that explain why output did not fall and unemployment did not rise.

First, there was an increase in $\chi_{t}$, which led to an endogenous reduction in labor force participation. This shift explains an even stronger backward movement of the AS curve, thus requiring a smaller increase in demand to account for the inflation surge. As we have shown, however, the characterization of the Phillips curve in terms of labor tightness means that $\theta_{t}$ is a sufficient statistic that incorporates the effect of labor force participation via $\chi_{t}$, which does not appear in the Phillips curve when expressed in terms of $\theta_{t}$. Therefore, variations in $\chi_{t}$ are only relevant to the extent that we aim to rationalize the output dynamics associated with the inflation surge. While it is plausible that $\chi_{t}$ increased during the COVID-19 epidemic, its reversal to pre-COVID levels is also observed in the data. Moreover, our assumption that this represents exogenous shifts seems largely plausible, as it was driven by factors related to the spread of the COVID-19 virus, the development of vaccines, treatments, and so forth.

Second, while the decline in labor force participation can be partly explained by an increase in $\chi_{t}$, this does not provide the full explanation for two reasons. First, not only did the labor force participation rate decline, but there was also a sharp increase in the separation rate between active workers and firms. Additionally, the data suggest a decline in matching efficiency: the number of matches created for a given number of posted vacancies and unemployed workers decreased. Both phenomena can be captured in reduced form by allowing for time variation in the parameters $s_{t}$ and $m_{t}$. However, $s_{t}$ does not represent the separation rate commonly used in the literature; instead, it denotes the fraction of the labor force that, at the beginning of a given period, is not attached to a job. This fraction either becomes unemployed or is hired into a job.

## Page 47

The analysis that follows illustrates how this extension sheds light on why $v_{t}/u_{t}$ does a better job of explaining the 2020s inflationary surge through labor market tightness rather than using only unemployment, and how it connects to the dynamic of the Beveridge curve.

Combining equation (25) and (26) implies a Beveridge curve:

$v_{t}=\left(\frac{s_{t}-u_{t}}{m_{t}u_{t}^{\eta}}\right)^{\frac{1}{1-\eta}}$ (56)

for $u_{t}\leq s_{t}$, which holds at any time $t$, unlike representing only a steady-state relationship as in many labor market models. This is due to the abstraction in our model that the labor force attached to existing jobs is independent of past employment history and is determined at the beginning of each period before workers search for jobs.

We can back out the time series for $s_{t}$ directly from the data since it has a natural empirical counterpart. It measures the fraction of the labor force that is not attached to existing firms at the beginning of the period. At the end of the period, the fraction of unattached workers must then equal the sum of: i) the fraction hired ($h_{t}$, i.e., hires as a fraction of labor force) in the period, and ii) the fraction that winds up unemployed ($u_{t}$, i.e., unemployment rate). Thus, $s_{t}=h_{t}+u_{t}$, and both $h_{t}$ and $u_{t}$ can be empirically measured. The number of new hires, $h_{t}$, encompasses three flow variables: new entrants to the labor force who become employed, unemployed individuals from the previous period who find employment, and currently employed workers who transition to new jobs – thereby representing job-to-job transitions. These transitions make up approximately 3/4 of all new hires in the U.S. labor market. Using the time series for $h_{t}$, $u_{t}$ and $v_{t}$, we can back out from equation (56) the time series for matching efficiency as

$m_{t}=\frac{h_{t}}{u_{t}^{\eta}v_{t}^{1-\eta}},$ (57)

in which we set $\eta=0.4$ as in *Blanchard, Domash and Summers (2022)*.

In the Appendix we show in the first panel of Figure 29 that the fraction of unattached workers, $s_{t}$, spikes at the beginning of COVID-19, as indicated by the red dot, since several firms laid off workers in response to the pandemic. It then falls abruptly, corresponding to the inflation surge marked by the two dashed lines. During the inflation surge, and through to the end of the sample in 2024 Q2, it smoothly drops to pre-COVID levels and has recently stabilized. The second panel of Figure 29 shows a sharp reduction in matching efficiency at the beginning of the pandemic, which is then partially reversed and gradually improves during the inflation surge. Unlike $s_{t}$, matching efficiency still remains below pre-COVID levels at the end of our sample.

To see how the evolution of these two variables helps explaining why $v/u$ is useful to characterize

the recent inflation dynamics, we can rewrite (56) as:

$\theta_{t}=\frac{v_{t}}{u_{t}}=\left\{\frac{s_{t}-u_{t}}{m_{t}u_{t}}\right\}^{\frac{1}{1-q}}$ (58)

If both $m_{t}$ and $s_{t}$ are fixed, then the Beveridge curve – equation (56) – gives a one-to-one mapping between unemployment and vacancies, or equivalently, between unemployment and $\theta$. This implies that there isn’t much to gain from replacing $u_{t}$ with $\frac{v_{t}}{u_{t}}$ in the empirical analysis. However, equation (58) clarifies that a tight labor market, e.g., $\theta>1$, can be consistent with high unemployment, as observed during the run-up of inflation in 2021, when the vacancy rate is also high. This situation can arise either due to a large number of unattached workers $s_{t}$ or because the matching efficiency $m_{t}$ is low. For instance, at the start of the inflation surge in May 2021, unemployment was at 5.75%, leading many observers to consider the labor market slack. However, the vacancy rate was 6.6%, implying $\theta=1.07>1$.

Figure 30 plots the Beveridge curve (56) since January 2020, reflecting the shifts in $s_{t}$ and $m_{t}$. It is notable how the data since March 2022 align well with a stable curve.

While the spike in separations and therefore unattached workers is clearly traced to the COVID-19 crisis, we believe that the same factors affected matching efficiency. A reasonable explanation for the decline in matching efficiency – indicated by the need for employment agencies to post more vacancies to generate an employment match for a given level of unemployment – is a structural change in aggregate spending. It likely takes a worker longer to find a suitable job match when moving across sectors than when switching jobs within the same sector. *Eggertsson and Kohn (2023)* demonstrate that the COVID-19 recovery was uneven, with spending in the goods market significantly outpacing the recovery in services. The spending mix has yet to return to its pre-COVID composition. While we have not explicitly modeled matching efficiency, an important extension of the framework would be to make matching efficiency endogenous. Another area for future research is extending the model to incorporate two sectors, reflecting the uneven increase in spending across sectors observed in the data, and to assume higher search costs for finding a job in a new sector.

In a followup paper, *Benigno and Eggertsson (2024)*, we extend our framework to achieve a more realistic analytical representation of the Beveridge curve by modeling variations in $s_{t}$ in greater detail. The bottom line is that the model captures the dynamics of the vacancy rate and unemployment rate reasonably well and predicts a soft landing.

5.2 Understanding the inflation surge in the 1970s, the disinflation and Volcker recession in the 1980s

We next consider how the model explains the increase in inflation during the 1970s, often referred to as the "Great Inflation." This narrative will be familiar to most readers, and our interpretation does not differ substantially from the conventional account (see, e.g., *Erceg and Levin, (2003)* and *Goodfriend and King, (2006)*). Nevertheless, it is helpful to clearly articulate this explanation within our model so that we can contrast it with our account of the inflationary surge of the 2020s. As in the previous section, it is useful to consider both the short run and the long run, defined by a stochastic reversal.

First, we assume a short-run supply shock given by $\hat{v}_{S}=(1-\alpha)\hat{q}_{S}$, meaning we consider the possibility that inflation was triggered by the large oil shocks observed during the 1970s. Second, we assume that in the short run, the public held different beliefs about the central bank’s long-run inflation target, which we denote as $\pi^{*}$. This implies that even though long-run inflation eventually stabilized (i.e., $\pi_{L}=\pi^{*}$), as was eventually the case in the U.S., the public’s expectations in the 1970s did not align with how things actually turned out. In other words, $\pi_{L}^{e}$ is assumed to differ from where inflation settles ex post.

The rationale for this assumption is straightforward: several long-term inflation expectation measures in the 1970s and early 1980s, as cited in the introduction, suggested that 5-year 5-year forward inflation expectations reached 10% and only gradually declined during the 1980s (see especially footnote 7). This indicates that the public’s long-run inflation expectations in the 1970s were "incorrect" ex post.

In the long run, we assume the labor market is back to "normal." In the absence of shocks, the unique bounded solution is simply given by $\pi_{L}=\pi^{*}$ and $\hat{Y}_{L}=0<\hat{Y}^{*}$.

Let us now consider the short run. Suppose that expected long-term inflation expectations are above $\pi^{*}$, as observed in the data, and constant at $\pi_{L}^{e}>\pi^{*}$. Then inflation expectations in the short run are

$E_{S}\pi_{S+1}=\tau\pi_{S}+(1-\tau)\pi_{L}^{e}.$

Since our model is characterized by long-run monetary policy neutrality, then even though people expect $\pi_{L}^{e}>\pi^{*}$, it remains the case that a unique bounded solution is given by $\hat{Y}_{L}=0$.

$\hat{Y}_{S}=-\sigma^{-1}\frac{\phi_{\pi}-\tau}{1-\tau}(\pi_{S}-\pi^{*})+\sigma^{-1}(\pi_{L}^{e}-\pi^{*}),$ (59)

Consider first the effect of a supply shock. This directly shifts the red curve upward, leading to higher inflation and lower output, as shown by the movement from $AS$ to $AS'$. Of greater importance, however, is the effect of a rise in the central bank's inflation target as perceived by the private sector and reflected in expected inflation, i.e., $\pi_{L}^{*} > \pi^{*}$. If the public believes that the central bank will set a higher inflation target in the long run, this results in a one-to-one upward shift of the red curve, from $AS'$ to $AS''$. We can use the same framework to understand why disinflation can be very costly if the private sector believes that the long-term inflation target is high, despite the central bank's claims to the contrary. The extent of this cost fundamentally depends on how perceptions about future inflation are formed. Consider the possibility that people will only reconsider their perception of the long-run inflation target if they see a significant reduction in current inflation. In this scenario, due to the flatness of the Phillips curve, it would be very costly to bring inflation down. This is a common narrative for the Volcker recession in the early 1980s. While we will not spell out this process explicitly here, it should be evident that we have all the main ingredients to tell that story by linking inflation expectations to realized output and inflation, which could be interpreted by the public as measures of the central bank's resolve in bringing down inflation.

One prominent hypothesis on the causes of inflation in the 1970s, proposed by Clarida, Galí, and Gertler (2000), is that the central bank did not react strongly enough to rising inflation by increasing the interest rate, implying $\phi_{\pi} < 1$. This assumption leads to equilibrium indeterminacy, meaning that there are infinite possible inflation paths that could satisfy the model's conditions. However, our focus here is on highlighting the effect of inflation expectations becoming unanchored. We are interested in comparative statics concerning the central bank's long-run inflation target. If the model allows for an infinite number of equilibria, comparative statics become meaningless. Therefore, it is useful to assume $\phi_{\pi} > 1$. Moreover, since we incorporate a policy shock $e_t$, the policy rule remains flexible enough to account for the possibility that monetary policy did not respond strongly enough to the rise in inflation, thereby capturing the essence of the argument by Clarida, Galí, and Gertler (2000).

# 5.3 The missing 2008 disinflation

As illustrated in Figure 11, our model effectively explains why inflation did not decrease significantly following the Financial Crisis of 2008. This outcome was often cited as a key limitation of the New Keynesian Phillips curve. While various authors have proposed alternative approaches to address this issue, our model replicates the observed inflation dynamic during this period without much difficulty.

In Figure 11, as shown in Appendix E, the zero lower bound causes the AD curve to become upward sloping at low inflation rates. We begin at the initial equilibrium, represented by point E, where inflation is at the  $2\%$  target and the output gap is stabilized at zero. At this equilibrium, the real interest rate is  $1\%$ . To define the shock characterizing the 2009 financial crisis, we follow the literature, such as Eggertsson and Krugman (2012), and hypothesize a negative shock to the natural rate of interest, reducing it to  $-4\%$ . With the zero lower bound binding, the upward sloping segment of the AD curve becomes relevant, determining the new equilibrium at point  $\mathbf{E}^{\prime}$ , where it intersects the flat segment of the AS curve. As a result, inflation falls to  $1\%$ , a level observed in the U.S. economy during the financial crisis for core CPI inflation, while output contracts significantly. The flat Phillips curve under weak labor market conditions explains why this shock did not lead to a more substantial reduction in the inflation rate or cause deflation.

# 6 Conclusion

In this paper we have proposed a reformulation of what has become known as the canonical New Keynesian Phillips curve and replaced it with one that admits significant nonlinearities. Our hypothesis is that the nonlinearity is responsible for the increase in inflation in the 2020s. We conjecture that a key reason why policymakers and market participants alike failed to foresee the surge in inflation, or its persistence is that they implicitly or explicitly assumed a "flat" Phillips curve. Even after substantial inflation had already occurred, the reassurance that expectations were holding stable further induced the belief that the surge was merely transitory. One question is why the Federal Reserve did not raise interest rates more quickly. Possibly the new policy framework announced in 2020 put greater emphasis on the employment side of the Fed's dual objective. Yet, at the same time, it acknowledged that there was no agreement on any precise measure of how close the US economy was to full employment at any given point in time. This, of course, contrasts very sharply with the other side of the mandate, i.e. inflation, for which there is broad consensus on how the Fed can attain its objective.

Figure 12 sheds some light on why policymakers may have believed in 2021 that even though the traditional gauge of labor slack, i.e. unemployment, was very low, this did not capture the full picture. The unemployment rate only tells us how many active job seekers there are. As the figure reveals, however, participation collapsed with the COVID-19 epidemic, which might have suggested to many that there was still considerable room for employment to grow further. Moreover, given the flat Phillips curve – the professional consensus at the time – and stable inflation expectations, it might have been tempting for policymakers to explore the possibility that the US economy could attract greater labor force participation, e.g. similar to pre-pandemic level with relatively low risk of inflation. In terms of the dual mandate, conditional on a flat Phillips curve, this could easily have been

## Page 54

seen at the time as a situation with possible high reward and relatively limited downside risk. The bottom line of this paper, however, is that the inflationary risk of allowing the labor market to tighten too much, to a degree we have defined as labor shortage, generates much greater upside risk for inflation than has been commonly thought. An important reason for this underestimation of inflation risk is no doubt the unprecedented labor shortage, historically unprecedented except in wartime, and the countless estimates of the slope of the Phillips curve that did not incorporate wartime. We have sought first to show this empirically and then to build a model to explain it. The good news, in any case, is that if our theory is correct the cost of taming inflation triggered by a labor shortage, but with stable inflation expectations, can be expected to be much lower than it was in the 1970s.

B Appendix: empirical results using Kalman-filter estimation

As an alternative benchmark to capture nonlinearities, we consider a specification that allows for time-varying coefficients, focusing on the period from 2008 Q3 to 2024 Q2. Generally, the results support our previous findings. We follow closely the existing literature, see *Blanchard et al. (2015)*. We consider the regression reported in Table 1, but the parameters are now allowed to vary over time by a random walk. The model is estimated by a Kalman filter using as initial conditions the OLS estimates generated by a regression up to 2008 Q2. Figure 13 shows how the estimated coefficients vary over time from 2008 Q3 to 2024 Q2 with red lines. The blue lines correspond to one-standard-deviation confidence bands.

The main conclusion is that the estimated coefficients shift sharply towards the end of the sample, once $\theta>1$. The slope of the curve steepens significantly in the post-COVID period, ending with a value close to 2. This is consistent with the results in Table 1, although smaller in magnitude. The coefficient on the supply shock also increases from zero to over 0.18. This is of the same order, even if slightly smaller, than the estimated value of the supply shock in Table 1 when $\theta>1$.

The inflation-persistence coefficient declines over time and hovers near zero at end of the sample. This, too, is one of our model’s main predictions when $\theta>1$, and is consistent with the benchmark regression.

Figure 14 highlights how poorly a forecaster would have done using either our benchmark regression or our regression with time varying coefficients if the forecaster fails to incorporate the non-linearities. The results reported in that figure give a natural explanation for why both policy makers and professional forecasters consistently failed to forecast the scope of the surge in inflation, as well as its persistence, as shown in Figures 16 and 17 already mentioned in the introduction.

D Appendix: Data Description

Table 1 presents the estimates of equation

$\pi_{t}=\beta_{c}+\beta_{\pi}\pi_{t-1}+(\beta_{\theta}+\beta_{\theta_{d}}D_{t})\ln\theta_{t}+(\beta_{\nu}+\beta_{\nu_{d}}D_{t})\cdot\nu_{t}+\beta_{\pi^{e}}\cdot\pi^{e}_{t}+\varepsilon_{t},$ (D.1)

in which $\pi_{t}$ is the annualized quarterly inflation rate, computed as log changes, in deviation from a 2% inflation target. The rate is computed using the CPI core component (net of energy and food); $\pi_{t-1}$ is its lagged value. Data on CPI are from FRED, collected quarterly, using the average of monthly observations for each quarter.

$\ln\theta_{t}$ is the log of the ratio of vacancies to unemployed workers provided by *Barnichon (2011)* and updated by the author. Data are monthly. Accordingly, the quarterly series used in the regression is log of the average of the relevant monthly observations. $D_{t}$ is a dummy variable taking the value one when $\theta_{t}\geq 1$.

$\nu_{t}$ is the four-quarter average of the principal component of the following three series: headline shocks, both to CPI and PCE, and import shock. The CPI or PCE headline shock is the difference between the annualized quarterly inflation rate computed using the CPI or PCE price index and that computed using the CPI or PCE price index excluding energy and food. The import shock is the difference between the annualized quarterly inflation rate computed using the import-price deflator and that computed using the GDP deflator. Data are from FRED and collected quarterly, using the average of the relevant monthly observations. We proxy the supply shock with the four-quarter average of the principal component of the three series. Let $z_{t}$ be the principal component of the three series described above; then $\nu_{t}$ is given by:

$\nu_{t}=(z_{t}+z_{t-1}+z_{t-2}+z_{t-3})/4.$

We proxy inflation expectations ($\pi^{e}$) with the 2-year inflation expectations of the Federal Reserve Bank of Cleveland, which are collected from FRED quarterly and available since 1982 Q2. The series is patched backward to 1960 Q1 with the 12-month inflation expectations from the Livingston survey. Since the latter is twice yearly, missing observations are interpolated though a spline curve-preserving function. In all regressions, $\pi_{t}$ and $\pi^{e}_{t}$ are deviations with respect to a 2% annual inflation target.

### E.1 Derivation of the AS equation (23)

$y_{t}(i)=A_{t}(N_{t}^{ex}(i)+N_{t}^{new}(i))^{\alpha}O_{t}(i)^{1-\alpha},$ (E.7)

### F.3 The 2008 missing disinflation

To characterize the 2008 missing disinflation, we consider a shock to the natural rate of interest that brings the policy rate at the zero-lower bound. The shock reverts to zero in the long run. This is an absorbing state that occurs with probability $1-\tau.$ In the long run $\hat{Y}_{L}=0$ and $\pi_{L}=\pi^{*}.$

Note that the interest rate policy is bounded by the zero lower bound and then given by

$\hat{\imath}_{S}=\max(\rho\hat{r}_{S}^{e}+\phi_{\pi}(\pi_{S}-\pi^{*}),-\ln(1+i)),$

in which $i$ is the steady-state interest rate. Under the condition

$\hat{r}_{S}^{e}>\frac{\phi_{\pi}(\pi^{*}-\pi_{S})-\ln(1+i)}{\rho}$

the interest rate is positive and the zero lower bound is not binding. In this case, the short-run aggregate demand is given by

$\hat{Y}_{S}=\tau\hat{Y}_{S}-\sigma^{-1}(\pi^{*}+(\rho-1)\hat{r}_{S}^{e}+\phi_{\pi}(\pi_{S}-\pi^{*})-\tau\pi_{S}-(1-\tau)\pi^{*}),$

which implies:

$\hat{Y}_{S}=\sigma^{-1}\frac{(1-\rho)\hat{r}_{S}^{e}}{1-\tau}-\sigma^{-1}\frac{\phi_{\pi}-\tau}{1-\tau}(\pi_{S}-\pi^{*}).$ (F.34)

On the contrary, when the zero lower bound is binding, the $AD$ equation is

$\hat{Y}_{S}=\tau\hat{Y}_{S}-\sigma^{-1}(\pi^{*}-\ln(1+i)-\tau\pi_{S}-(1-\tau)\pi^{*}-\hat{r}_{S}^{e})$

and therefore

$\hat{Y}_{S}=\sigma^{-1}\frac{\ln(1+i)+\hat{r}_{S}^{e}}{1-\tau}+\sigma^{-1}\frac{\tau}{1-\tau}(\pi_{S}-\pi^{*})$ (F.35)

showing a positive relationship between output and inflation.

The AS equation characterizing the missing disinflation is that on the flat part of the inv-L NK Phillips curve and is given by:

$\pi_{S}=\tilde{\kappa}\hat{Y}_{S}+\tau\pi_{S}+(1-\tau)\pi^{*}$
